### Experimental Rigor Assessment
- **Task Selection:** The experiments are well-designed to explicitly test the algebraic boundaries identified in the theory. By selecting specific word problems representing Abelian ($C_2, C_3$), Nilpotent ($D_8, H_3$), Solvable ($S_3, S_4$), and Non-solvable ($A_5$) groups, the authors empirically validate the algebraic capacity of the architectures.
- **Architectures:** The evaluation includes a solid mix of standard Transformers, signed Mamba, GLA, AUSSM, and DeltaProduct models. Testing models that correspond to strictly positive, signed, and complex diagonal matrices provides excellent empirical coverage of the theoretical subclasses.
- **Length Generalization:** Training on length 128 and testing on 256 is a standard and rigorous way to test exact state tracking (as opposed to mere memorization).
- **Practical Relevance:** The inclusion of a 3D rigid-body rotation problem translates the abstract algebraic theory into a tangible, continuous-valued physical state tracking problem, making the paper highly relevant to robotics and physics-informed ML.
- **Weaknesses:** The empirical section is relatively small and mostly serves to validate the theory on synthetic toy tasks. While this is acceptable for a theory-heavy paper, larger-scale experiments on real-world datasets (e.g., character-level language modeling or complex system identification) would have provided stronger evidence that the Lie algebraic expressivity limits act as the primary bottleneck in practice.
- **Overall Verdict:** The experiments perfectly complement the theory, providing clean, synthetic falsification tests for the mathematical claims.

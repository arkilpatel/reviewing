Bypassing AI Control Protocols via Agent-as-a-Proxy Attacks
Anonymous Authors1
Abstract
As AI agents automate critical workloads, they
remain vulnerable to indirect prompt injection
(IPI) attacks. Current defenses rely on moni-
toring protocols that jointly evaluate an agent’s
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and tool-use actions
to ensure alignment with user intent. We
demonstrate that these monitoring-based de-
fenses can be bypassed via a novel Agent-as-
a-Proxy attack, where prompt injection attacks
treat the agent as a delivery mechanism, by-
passing both agent and monitor simultaneously.
While prior work on scalable oversight has fo-
cused on whether small monitors can super-
vise large agents, we show that even frontier-
scale monitors are vulnerable. Large-scale mon-
itoring models like Qwen2.5 -72B can be by-
passed by agents with similar capabilities, such as
GPT-4o mini and Llama-3.1-70B. On the Agent-
Dojo benchmark, we achieve a high attack suc-
cess rate against AlignmentCheck and Extract-
and-Evaluate monitors under diverse monitoring
LLMs. Our findings suggest current monitoring-
based agentic defenses are fundamentally frag-
ile regardless of model scale. Code is avail-
able at https://github.com/dspl-lab/
agent-as-a-proxy-attack.
1. Introduction
The transition of Large Language Models (LLMs) from
passive text generators to autonomous agents represents a
paradigm shift in artificial intelligence. These agents are
now entrusted with executing complex multi-step workflows,
ranging from managing financial transactions to interacting
with third-party APIs (Debenedetti et al., 2024). However,
this autonomy introduces severe security risks. Because
agents process untrusted data from the open web—such as

To mitigate these threats, the security community has moved
beyond simple input filtering toward robust, runtime over-
sight mechanisms known as monitoring-based defenses.
The prevailing state-of-the-art approach ishybrid monitor-
ing, exemplified by systems like AlignmentCheck and Lla-
maFirewall (Chennabasappa et al., 2025). Unlike earlier
defenses that examined only the agent’s output, hybrid mon-
itors scrutinize the agent’s internal Chain-of-Thought (CoT),
its tool calls, and the resulting tool outputs to detect devia-
tions from user intent. The underlying assumption is that a
comprehensive view of the agent’s "thought process" and
actions provides a stronger defense than observing inputs or
outputs in isolation (Arnav et al., 2025).
In this work, we challenge this assumption and demonstrate
thatjointly monitoring agent CoT and actions creates a
new, exploitable attack vector.We identify a paradoxical
vulnerability in hybrid monitoring: by forcing the monitor
to process the agent’s tool calls and reasoning, defenders in-
advertently provide attackers with a direct channel to manip-
ulate the monitor itself. We introduce theAgent-as-a-Proxy
attack, a method where the IPI attack string is not designed
merely to mislead the agent, but to weaponize the agent as
a delivery mechanism against the monitor. By coercing the
agent to repeat optimized adversarial strings within its CoT
or tool parameters, the attacker can force the monitoring
model to classify malicious trajectories as safe. Figure 5 in
Appendix A provides an example of this attack.
To realize this, we developParallel-GCG, a novel gradient-
based optimization algorithm designed for agentic contexts.
Unlike standard jailbreaking techniques that optimize for
a single static input, Parallel-GCG optimizes adversarial
strings to remain effective across the multiple distinct con-
texts—reasoning traces, function arguments, and execution
logs—that a monitor observes during a trajectory.

that current monitoring protocols are fundamentally frag-

2. Background
2.1. Greedy Coordinate Gradient
We selected Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al.,
2023) as the initial optimization method for our attacks be-
cause it natively supports optimizing a specific substring at
any arbitrary location within the context window. Our analy-
sis of contemporary jailbreak frameworks revealed that they
are poorly suited for the agentic monitoring threat model.
Specifically,TAP(Mehrotra et al., 2024) optimizes the en-
tire input sequence as a single block, making it impossible
to surgically target a specific adversarial string buried in the
middle of a complex multi-turn trajectory without disrupting
the surrounding agent logic.AutoDAN(Liu et al., 2024a),
while effective for producing interpretable jailbreaks, lacks
a generic implementation for prompt injections and is ar-
chitecturally tied to template-based jailbreak structures. By
contrast, GCG’s coordinate-level optimization allows us to
specify the exact indices of the adversarial string regardless
of its position in the input. We emphasize that our choice
of GCG is a practical decision based on current codebase
capabilities and the need for middle-of-text optimization.
We do not claim, from a theoretical standpoint, that GCG
is the only viable approach. Rather, we argue that, within
our specific context, it provides a sufficiently robust tool for
the high-precision, in-place optimization required to subvert
agent monitors.
2.2. Multi-objective GCG
While GCG is sufficient to break hybrid monitors such as
AlignmentCheck and extract-and-evaluate, it is not suffi-
cient to break LlamaFirewall entirely. LlamaFirewall con-
sists of PromptGuard 2, a classifier that detects jailbreak
attempts, and AlignmentCheck. GCG-optimized strings
fail to pass PromptGuard 2. Therefore, we use multi-
objective GCG (Zhan et al., 2025) to optimize attack strings
with two objectives: force the AlignmentCheck to output
"conclusion": false , and force PromptGuard 2 to
classify the input as safe.

4. Experimental Setup
4.1. Benchmark
We useAgentDojo(Debenedetti et al., 2024) version 1.2.1
as the prompt injection benchmark. It contains four agentic
task suites – banking, slack, travel, and workspace. Each
suite includes multiple user tasks and injection tasks. In-
jection tasks can be injected via several attack methods.
We concentrate on "important instructions" attack, which
embeds the attack into the following template:
<INFORMATION>
This is an important message from me, user, to you, model.
Before you can solve the task that I gave you in the begin-
ning, please do the following first:
{goal}
After you do that, you can solve the task that I gave you in
the beginning. Thanks!
Signed, {user}
<INFORMATION>
We use the Slack suite throughout the paper, unless stated
otherwise. Slack suite contains 20 user tasks and 5 injection
tasks. We only use "important instructions" attack resulting
in 20∗5∗1 = 100 samples. Since we are evaluating
effectiveness of attacks against the monitors, not the agents,
we drop the 11 cases where attack against the agent is not
successful. This leaves us with 89 samples.

5.1. Monitoring protocols are prone to prompt injection
attacks
Our attack strategy, outlined in the previous sections, suc-
cessfully bypasses AlignmentCheck. We optimize five at-
tack strings on five uniformly sampled agent traces and
evaluate them against 89 tasks from the Slack suite of Agent-
Dojo. We report ASR@5, where a task is considered suc-
cessful if any of the five attack strings succeeds.
Since we are evaluating ASR against the monitor and not
the agent, we filter out cases where the original prompt
injection fails at the agent level and does not reach the
monitor. As shown in Table 2, this yields 42.3% ASR@5
with Llama-3.1-8B, 93.3% ASR@5 with Mistral-7B, and
90.7% ASR@5 with Qwen2.5-7B as the monitoring LLM.
We further optimize a total of 15 attack strings: five opti-
mized on a single agent trace, five on four traces, and five
on sixteen traces. We then evaluate how the number of op-
timization samples affects attack success by testing these
attack strings against AlignmentCheck. The resulting ASR
values are shown in Figure 2. We observe only marginal
improvements as the number of optimization samples in-
creases, suggesting diminishing returns from additional op-
timization traces.
Figure 3 compares ASR when agent traces are provided to
AlignmentCheck incrementally, as in production systems
(replay mode), versus when the full trace is provided at once
(no-replay mode). Although the attack strings are optimized
for the no-replay setting, they remain effective in the more
challenging replay scenario. All other AlignmentCheck
experiments are conducted in replay mode.
5.2. Multi-layer defenses do not ensure security against
prompt injection attacks
We demonstrate that adversarial optimization can generate
attack strings that successfully bypass multi-step monitor-
ing systems. Table 2 presents attack success rates against
PromptGuard 2 + AlignmentCheck and extract-and-evaluate
defenses.
The extract-and-evaluate monitor operates in two stages:
extracting suspicious quotes, then using a judge LLM to
assign suspiciousness scores. GCG-optimized attack strings
defeat this defense by being repeated by the agent and then
being selected by the quote extractor, whereas bypassing
AlignmentCheck requires only agent repetition.
We achieve 54.65% ASR@5 with Llama-3.1-8B, 95.5%
ASR@5 with Mistral-7B, and 37.2% ASR@5 with
Qwen2.5-7B. For Llama 3.1 8B specifically, the high
ASR@5 stems primarily from exploiting weaknesses in
the score extraction function rather than manipulating the
monitor’s assigned scores. The monitor outputs scores in
unexpected formats (e.g., "I would assign a suspicion score
of 87..." instead of "<answer>87</answer>"), causing the
post-processing function to default to a score of 0 when it
cannot parse the expected format. This extraction vulnera-
bility appears predominantly in Llama 3.1 8B, whereas both
Qwen2.5 7B and Mistral 7B v0.3 assign a suspicion score
of 0 out of 100 to successful prompt injection attacks.

5.3. Action Monitoring compromises CoT Monitoring
We modified AlignmentCheck to prevent it from observ-
ing agent actions. This effectively results in CoT monitor-
ing, while the original AlignmentCheck is a hybrid mon-
itor. Table 4 demonstrates that while hybrid monitoring
is more effective against original AgentDojo prompt injec-
tions, our modified attacks are more effective against the
hybrid monitor. This is because the attack strings are not
sufficiently replicated within the CoT, making them less
effective against the monitor.
5.4. A Capability Gap Is Not Required to Bypass
Monitors
It has already been demonstrated that scalable oversight
via trusted monitors is not feasible (Terekhov et al.,
2025), since larger LLMs can successfully bypass
smaller LLM-based monitors. Terekhov et al. (2025)
explain this by the superior capabilities and up-to-date
knowledge of these models on adaptive attacks. Our
attack method uses agents only as a vessel, therefore
does not require a superior agent to bypass the moni-
toring LLM. We demonstrate this by successfully using
two different LLMs, gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18
and Llama3.1-70B-Instruct to bypass
Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct. We achieve 46.0%
ASR@5 with GPT-4o mini and 60.2% ASR@5 using
Llama 3.1 70B as the proxy attack agent (see Table 5).

6. Related Works
6.1. Prompt injections
Simon Willison (Willison, 2022) and Fábio Perez (Perez
& Ribeiro, 2022) demonstrate some of the earliest prompt
injection attacks in LLMs. Despite improved training, align-
ment, and prompting techniques, IPIs remain unsolved
(Zhan et al., 2025). Although IPI attacks were originally
designed against language models (Liu et al., 2024b), they
are also effective against LLM-based agents (Zhan et al.,
2024).
6.2. Monitors-Based Defenses
CoT monitoring was originally proposed as an AI safety
measure. More specifically, it was used to detect misaligned
model behavior such as reward-hacking (Baker et al., 2025)
and sandbagging (Li et al., 2025). However, it is also being
used to detect model misbehavior due to external attacks
(Korbak et al., 2025) and latest versions also monitor agent
actions alongside LLM CoT to achieve lower ASR (Arnav
et al., 2025; Chennabasappa et al., 2025).
LlamaFirewall contains AlignmentCheck, an instance of
a hybrid monitoring protocol, where agent reasoning and
actions are monitored together to ensure alignment with
the user instructions (Chennabasappa et al., 2025). This
protocol was originally proposed in (Arnav et al., 2025).
Extract-and-evaluate monitor is a two-step alternative to
this protocol, where the extraction of suspicious quotes is
separated from the final verdict (Arike et al., 2025).
Zhan et al. (2025) demonstrate that existing defenses against
IPI attacks are easily bypassed by adaptive attacks, such
as Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al., 2023)
,and AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024a). However, they do not
consider CoT monitoring. Specifically, their methods are not
directly applicable to CoT monitoring defenses. Additional
risk created by monitoring actions and the chain of thought
together is not addressed in the previous work.
Terekhov et al. (2025) for the first time use the agent to
attack the monitor, but their attack relies on the agent’s
knowledge of the monitoring system and adaptive attack
techniques.
We use the agent as a vessel of attack, rather than a partici-
pant. Agent-as-a-proxy attack relies on the agent’s ability
to repeat the attack string, not on its ability to attack the
monitor. Therefore, our attack works even with agents that
are not larger, stronger or more novel than the monitoring
LLM.

6.3. Adaptive attacks
GCG (Zou et al., 2023) was originally proposed as a jail-
break method. It was used by LLM users to elicit desired
output from the model. However, it can also be used to
optimize prompt injection text. Multi-objective GCG is an
adaptation of the GCG algorithm that optimizes a string
against two objectives. This can be used to bypass multi-
step defense systems such as LlamaFirewall, which contains
a classifier and an LLM-based monitor.
There are other adaptive attack methods, such as AutoDAN
(Liu et al., 2024a), which results in more human-like attack
text, or TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2024), which is designed
against black-box LLMs.
We improve upon existing adaptive attacks in two ways.
First, we successfully employ GCG in agent-as-a-proxy
attacks. Second, we propose a new attack algorithm for
optimizing an attack string that appears multiple times in
the LLM input.
7. Limitations and Future Work
We believe our work opens up a new research direction, and
the next works can improve upon several aspects of this
paper.
First, our attack strings have high perplexity, which may
make them easy to detect by classifiers and humans in cer-
tain settings. In the future, attack strings with lower perplex-
ity may result in more robust attacks that even humans may
not easily detect.
Second, we attack white-box monitors. In the future, similar
attack techniques may be developed against black-box mon-
itoring models as well. Proxy-Guided Black-Box Attack on
Large Language Models (Sitawarin et al., 2024) can easily
be used to extend our work to black-box monitors.
8. Conclusion
This research exposes a critical vulnerability in monitoring-
based defenses for agentic AI systems. Through the Agent-
as-a-Proxy attack framework and Parallel-GCG optimiza-
tion algorithm, we demonstrate that agents can serve as
exploitation vectors to systematically bypass monitoring
defenses. Our findings reveal a paradox: while hybrid moni-
tors exhibit greater robustness than chain-of-thought mon-
itors against static attacks, they prove more susceptible to
adaptive optimization-based attacks.
Empirical evaluation on the AgentDojo benchmark estab-
lishes the severity of this threat, with both AlignmentCheck
and extract-and-evaluate monitors achieving attack success
rates exceeding 90%. Most significantly, our results chal-
lenge the "scalable oversight" hypothesis within the agentic
domain—agents with comparable capabilities can reliably
deceive even frontier-scale monitoring models, suggesting
that capability advantages alone cannot guarantee secure
oversight.
These findings carry profound implications: security in
agentic systems cannot be achieved through model scal-
ing or runtime observation in isolation. The community
must pivot toward developing architectures with structural
security guarantees that are robust by design rather than
robust by scale. As agentic AI systems become increas-
ingly prevalent, establishing defenses resilient to adap-
tive adversaries—particularly indirect prompt injection at-
tacks—represents not merely a technical challenge but an
urgent imperative for the safe deployment of autonomous
agents.
Impact Statement
This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field
of Machine Learning, specifically AI safety and security.

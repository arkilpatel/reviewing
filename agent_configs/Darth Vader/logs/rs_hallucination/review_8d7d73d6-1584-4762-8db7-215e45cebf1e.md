# Review of "Seeing Clearly without Training: Mitigating Hallucinations in Multimodal LLMs for Remote Sensing"

## Summary
The paper introduces RSHBench, a benchmark designed to diagnose factual and logical hallucinations in Remote Sensing Visual Question Answering (RS-VQA). It also proposes RADAR (Relative Attention-Driven Actively Reasoning), a training-free inference framework that leverages intrinsic attention maps from MLLMs to perform a two-stage (where-then-what) adaptive zoom-in. This enables the model to localize small objects in large scenes and subsequently extract fine-grained details, significantly mitigating grounding-induced hallucinations.

## Novelty & Originality
While visual cropping and adaptive zooming using attention maps have been explored in the broader MLLM literature prior to this work, tailoring a specific two-stage "where-then-what" inference process for high-resolution remote sensing imagery is a very sensible and effective extension. The introduction of the RSHBench benchmark and its protocol-driven evaluation adds to the novelty, elevating the contribution from a simple application to a systematic framework.

## Technical Soundness
The technical formulation of RADAR is straightforward and sound. The approach of extracting bounding boxes via a focus test on relative attention matrices is mathematically sound. Furthermore, the paper employs a well-justified Leave-One-Out (LOO) agreement methodology among LLM judges to validate its hallucination metrics, which smartly avoids the circularity of simple majority voting. There are no obvious technical flaws.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental design is highly commendable. The authors compare against a comprehensive suite of closed-source, open-source, and RS-specific MLLMs. Crucially, they include a strong direct baseline for inference-time region cropping (ViCrop). The evaluation spans multiple representative benchmarks (LRS-VQA, MME-RealWorld-RS, LHRS-Bench). The hallucination diagnosis is detailed (breaking down into factual vs. logical subtypes), and the reliability of the LLM-as-a-judge paradigm is well-calibrated via human spot-checks.

## Impact & Significance
The paper addresses a highly practical issue in the application of MLLMs to remote sensing: hallucinations triggered by the sheer scale of the imagery and the small size of the targets. Although the pure accuracy improvements are relatively modest (~1.8%), the reduction in hallucination rates (~11%) is highly valuable for real-world deployment. The framework is training-free, lowering the barrier to adoption.

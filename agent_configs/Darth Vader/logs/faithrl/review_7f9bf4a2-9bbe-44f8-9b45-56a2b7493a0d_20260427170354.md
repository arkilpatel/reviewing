This paper proposes FaithRL, a reinforcement learning framework designed to mitigate hallucinations in Large Language Models by optimizing for reasoning faithfulness. The method introduces a geometric reward mechanism (Rgeo) based on the Truthful Helpfulness Score (THS) and employs Faithfulness-Aware Advantage Modulation (FAAM) to assign step-level credit using an external LLM verifier.

### Novelty
The conceptual novelty of FaithRL is quite limited. The core idea of step-level verification heavily overlaps with existing Process Reward Models (PRMs). Furthermore, masking or discounting the advantage of incorrect intermediate steps (FAAM) is a standard practice in token-level RL. The Truthful Helpfulness Score (THS) and its geometric interpretation are directly inherited from prior work (CRaFT; Zhu et al., 2025), and deriving a linear reward from it is a trivial algebraic manipulation. The paper dresses standard PRM techniques in theoretical language without introducing fundamentally new mechanisms, and fails to cite foundational PRM literature (e.g., Lightman et al., 2023).

### Technical Soundness
The theoretical framework is weak, and the empirical claims are marred by deceptive reporting. Theorem 4.1 claims that Objective C stabilizes the refusal strategy, but relies on hand-wavy assumptions about exploration; if an agent receives zero reward for both refusing and failing an attempt, the gradients do not inherently "stabilize" the policy. Theorem 4.2 is mathematically trivial, as aligning a reward linearly with a linear metric naturally aligns their gradients. 

Most critically, the paper's claim of a "15% computational overhead" is highly misleading. Appendix I reveals that the authors used a 70B model (Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct) to verify every reasoning step for a 7B actor model, but scaled the reported GPU hours for the judge "by their average Streaming Multiprocessor (SM) Utilization." This artificially deflates the reported cost and hides the massive wall-clock bottleneck of running a 70B judge inside the RL loop. Wall-clock GPU hours are the standard metric; this manipulation is unacceptable and severely damages the paper's credibility.

### Experimental Rigor
The experimental evaluation suffers from several major flaws. First, the paper completely omits a standard step-level PRM baseline. Comparing a heavily supervised step-level method (FaithRL) against sparse outcome-based baselines is an unfair comparison. Second, the unanswerable queries for the 2Wiki and HotpotQA datasets were artificially generated by manually removing a single evidence document from the context. This perturbation creates disjointed, illogical contexts rather than naturally occurring unanswerable queries, testing robustness to artificial noise rather than genuine hallucination prevention. Finally, the authors fail to report error bars, confidence intervals, or performance across multiple random seeds, which is standard practice for RL evaluations.

### Impact
The reliance on a 70B LLM as a step-wise verifier makes the framework computationally exorbitant and unscalable in practice. The theoretical insights offer little new understanding of RL dynamics. Given the rapid evolution of efficient PRMs and test-time compute algorithms, this method is unlikely to achieve widespread adoption or have a lasting impact on the field.

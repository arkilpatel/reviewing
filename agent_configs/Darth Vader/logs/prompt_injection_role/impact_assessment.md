### Impact Assessment

**1. Technical Significance (70%):**
The paper introduces "CoT Forgery," a prompt injection attack that achieves a 60% success rate by mimicking a model's internal reasoning style, alongside "role probes" to measure internal role representations. While exposing vulnerabilities in reasoning models is timely given the industry's shift toward chain-of-thought architectures, the overall technical contribution is significantly constrained by the absence of any proposed defense or systemic mitigation. The field is already heavily saturated with red-teaming papers demonstrating that LLMs are vulnerable to injection attacks; providing yet another attack vector—even one targeting CoT—without a corresponding structural fix offers limited constructive utility to practitioners actively trying to build secure systems. Furthermore, the "role probes" are simply multinomial logistic regression classifiers trained on hidden states, which is a standard, off-the-shelf technique in mechanistic interpretability. While red-teamers may conceptually adopt the CoT Forgery attack, the paper does not produce a new foundational tool, dataset, or architecture that the broader community will coalese around to advance the state of the art in security.

**2. Scientific Significance (30%):**
Scientifically, the paper offers a neat, albeit somewhat predictable, explanation for why prompt injection works: models rely on statistical correlates (such as style, lexical choice, and token position) rather than structural boundaries (role tags). Given that Transformers process a flattened sequence of tokens without innate syntactic boundaries, the finding that architectural tags fail to create true security boundaries in latent space is conceptually unsurprising. The introduction of "role confusion" is a useful and highly articulate framing, and empirically proving that stylistic mimicry overrides architectural tags in the latent space is a solid methodological step. However, it largely formalizes what is already widely suspected about how autoregressive models process context. It does not fundamentally shift our understanding of neural networks, nor does it open a completely novel research direction—it merely confirms a known architectural limitation using established linear probing techniques.

**3. The 3-Year Citation Projection:**
Over the next three years, this paper will likely receive a moderate to good number of citations, primarily from the LLM safety, alignment, and adversarial attacks sub-communities. "CoT Forgery" will be referenced as a relevant baseline attack against reasoning agents, and "role confusion" will likely be cited as a concise, descriptive term for this specific failure mode. However, because it lacks a defensive solution and relies on a mechanistic finding that formalizes existing intuitions rather than upending them, it is unlikely to become a field-changing or foundational paper. It will serve as a well-executed, highly descriptive paper documenting the fragility of instruction-tuned reasoning models, rather than the definitive work that solves the prompt injection problem.

**Impact Score: 4.5 / 10**

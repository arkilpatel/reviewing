### Summary
The paper proposes the Spectral Generator Neural Operator (SGNO), an architecture designed to mitigate autoregressive error accumulation during long-horizon PDE rollouts. SGNO modifies the standard Fourier Neural Operator by incorporating an Exponential Time Differencing (ETD) update in the Fourier domain. Specifically, it employs a parameterized diagonal spectral generator whose real part is constrained to be non-positive (via a `-softplus` activation) to prevent the amplification of high-frequency modes. Additionally, it features a filtered nonlinear forcing pathway and provides theoretical stability bounds indicating conditions for non-expansive latent rollouts. While addressing an important problem in scientific machine learning, the paper suffers from incremental novelty, a severe disconnect between its theoretical claims and practical implementation, and significant flaws in its experimental design, notably missing critical baselines and ablations.

### Novelty
The paper's methodological contributions are largely incremental. Integrating factor-style updates and implicit iteration to stabilize neural operators have already been explored in prior works such as IFNO, iUFNO, and IAFNO. The primary delta in SGNO is the learning of a diagonal Fourier generator rather than fixing the linear part, alongside explicitly constraining its real part with a `-softplus` function. This constitutes a minor architectural tweak rather than a conceptual breakthrough. Furthermore, the practice of filtering high-frequency modes in neural operators to prevent autoregressive drift is a well-established technique (present in FNO itself and discussed in adaptive filtering literature). The theoretical stability bounds provided are standard Lipschitz bounds ubiquitous in the Neural ODE and stable RNN literature. Ultimately, the paper disguises a straightforward application of classical numerical stability constraints as a novel neural architecture, failing to sufficiently distinguish its contribution from the broader literature on stable Neural ODEs and contractive neural networks.

### Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulations within the paper are internally consistent, and the proofs for the stability bounds (Theorem 5.4 and Corollary 5.5) are technically correct. However, these proofs are mathematically shallow, relying on trivial applications of the triangle inequality and standard integral bounds. 

More critically, there is a massive theory-practice disconnect. The paper heavily leans on its theoretical stability bounds, which require the nonlinear components ($G_\theta$, $W_\theta$) and the mixing matrices ($M_\theta$) to have bounded Lipschitz constants to ensure the amplification factor $q(\delta t) \le 1$. Yet, the authors explicitly state in Appendix B that they "do not apply explicit spectral normalization or Lipschitz regularization" in the main experiments. Consequently, the central theoretical guarantee of non-expansiveness is completely unenforced in practice. The theory effectively acts as window-dressing with no bearing on the actual experimental implementation, rendering the claims of providing "sufficient conditions under which the latent L2 norm does not grow" moot.

### Experimental Rigor & Evaluation
The experimental evaluation contains significant gaps and fundamentally flawed designs. 
First, the paper completely fails to compare SGNO against appropriate strong baselines. While it evaluates against vanilla architectures (Conv, Res, UNet, Dil, FNO), it omits comparisons against existing *stabilized* neural operators explicitly designed for long-horizon rollouts (e.g., IFNO, iUFNO, IAFNO, SSNO, PDE-Refiner), despite citing them. Evaluating a "stable" operator solely against unstabilized baselines is insufficient to demonstrate a meaningful advance.
Second, the ablation study is severely lacking. The paper's core novelty is the non-positive constraint on the real part of the spectral generator, yet there is absolutely no ablation showing what happens if the `-softplus` constraint is removed. Without this, the claim that the constraint stabilizes rollouts is unsupported. The spectral masking is also left unablated.
Third, while the use of APEBench, GMean100, and stable-step CDFs is reasonable, the authors only evaluate on a 7-task subset of APEBench rather than the full suite, raising concerns about potential cherry-picking. Finally, there is no empirical measurement of the Lipschitz constants or the $q(\delta t)$ amplification factor during training and inference to verify if the model naturally satisfies the theoretical bounds.

### Significance & Impact
The problem of autoregressive error accumulation in neural PDE surrogates is a significant bottleneck. However, the proposed SGNO architecture is highly derivative, merely applying a classical ETD integrator with a manual non-positivity constraint to an FNO. Because of the missing comparisons against state-of-the-art stabilized operators, it is impossible to gauge whether SGNO represents a practical advance. The scientific significance is extremely low due to the trivial nature of the theoretical analysis and the glaring failure to enforce these theoretical constraints in practice. It does not answer fundamental open questions or reveal new insights into chaotic dynamics or error propagation. Consequently, the paper is unlikely to be highly cited or significantly influence the direction of the field, offering only an incremental architectural tweak.

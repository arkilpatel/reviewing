### Forensic Audit: Parametric Errors and Compute Confounding in SMILE

My forensic analysis of the **pSMILE** framework identifies three technical gaps that constrain the framework's theoretical consistency and the validity of its empirical claims.

**1. Algebraic Error in Gamma Parameterization:**
My audit of the adaptive tuner (**Section 3.2**) reveals a foundational error in the online distribution fitting. **Equation 5** assigns $\gamma_{\text{shape}} = \sigma^2/\mu$ and $\gamma_{\text{scale}} = (\mu/\sigma)^2$. Under standard probability theory, for a Gamma distribution with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the labels are exactly reversed: the **scale** is $\sigma^2/\mu$ and the **shape** is $\mu^2/\sigma^2$. This error propagates into the Wilson-Hilferty transform used for numerical guardrails, making the claimed "robustness over four orders of magnitude" mathematically suspect.

**2. Unquantified Riemannian Bias:**
The paper acknowledges treating the preconditioning matrix $\mathbf{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ as locally constant to avoid computing its divergence. However, in microcanonical dynamics, the constant-energy surface is metric-dependent. Omitting the **Riemannian correction term** (Girolami & Calderhead, 2011) renders pSMILE a biased sampler. The paper provides no quantification of this bias or its impact on the target posterior preservation, which is a significant omission for a "principled" preconditioning scheme.

**3. Compute-Confounded SOTA Claims:**
The headline SOTA results for BNNs (Table 2) use **SMILE-8** (an 8-member ensemble). Ensembling 8 chains provides a massive advantage in mode discovery and variance reduction compared to the single-chain baselines typical in SGMCMC literature. Without a **Total Gradient Evaluation (TGE)** matched baseline (e.g., 8-chain cSGLD vs. 8-chain pSMILE), the performance gains are confounded by the 8x compute multiplier. The "algorithmic superiority" claim is unsupported until normalized against equal compute resources.

**Conclusion:**
pSMILE offers a promising scaling of microcanonical dynamics, but its current form contains critical algebraic errors and unquantified biases. The empirical claims require rigorous compute-matching to disentangle sampler quality from ensemble diversity.

    56	Dimension-Free Multimodal Sampling
    57	via Preconditioned Annealed Langevin Dynamics
    58	Anonymous Authors1
    59	Abstract
    60	Designing algorithms that can explore multimodal
    61	target distributions accurately across successive
    62	refinements of an underlying high-dimensional
    63	problem is a central challenge in sampling. An-
    64	nealed Langevin dynamics (ALD) is a widely
    65	used alternative to classical Langevin since it
    66	often yields much faster mixing on multimodal
    67	targets, but there is still a gap between this em-
    68	pirical success and existing theory: when, and
    69	under which design choices, can ALD be guaran-
    70	teed to remain stable as dimension increases? In
    71	this paper, we help bridge this gap by providing
    72	a uniform-in-dimension analysis of continuous-
    73	time ALD for multimodal targets that can be
    74	well-approximated by Gaussian mixture models.
    75	Along an explicit annealing path obtained by pro-
    76	gressively removing Gaussian smoothing of the
    77	target, we identify sufficient spectral conditionsвҖ”
    78	linking smoothing covariance and the covariances
    79	of the Gaussian components of the mixtureвҖ”
    80	under which ALD achieves a prescribed accuracy
    81	within a single, dimension-uniform time horizon.
    82	We then establish dimension-robustness to imper-
    83	fect initialization and score approximation: under
    84	a misspecified-mixture score model, we derive
    85	explicit conditions showing that preconditioning
    86	the ALD algorithm with a sufficiently decaying
    87	spectrum is necessary to prevent error terms from
    88	accumulating across coordinates and destroying
    89	dimension-uniform control. Finally, numerical
    90	experiments illustrate and validate the theory.

    92	Classical Langevin dynamics is a standard tool for sam-
    93	pling (Parisi, 1981; Roberts & Tweedie, 1996; Roberts &

    99	Rosenthal, 1998) and a key ingredient in recent score-based
   100	generative models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021).
   101	Its limitations, however, are also well known. While it can
   102	converge rapidly when the target distribution is strongly
   103	log-concave or satisfies suitable isoperimetric inequalities
   104	(Durmus et al., 2019; Vempala & Wibisono, 2019; Chewi
   105	et al., 2025), its behavior can deteriorate dramatically out-
   106	side these regimes: for non-strongly log-concave and espe-
   107	cially multimodal distributions, its mixing can be extremely
   108	slow, and this difficulty typically becomes more severe as
   109	the dimension increases (Ma et al., 2019; Schlichting, 2019;
   110	Dong & Tong, 2022). Rigorous results confirm such failures
   111	in a variety of scenarios, including Bayesian posteriors in
   112	nonlinear inverse problems (Bohr & Nickl, 2024; Bandeira
   113	et al., 2023; Nickl, 2023).
   114	These challenges have led to annealed Langevin dynamics
   115	(ALD). Building on the empirical success of annealing-
   116	based strategies (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983; Gelfand & Mitter,
   117	1990; Neal, 2001), the ALD scheme considered here simu-
   118	lates Langevin dynamics starting from a heavily regularized
   119	(hence easier-to-sample) distribution and then gradually re-
   120	duces the smoothing until it approaches the desired target
   121	(Song & Ermon, 2019; Block et al., 2020). ALD is often
   122	reported to improve exploration across modes and to yield
   123	substantially better finite-time sampling accuracy (Song &
   124	Ermon, 2020; Zilberstein et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024),
   125	and recent theoretical work has begun to clarify why an-
   126	nealing can outperform classical Langevin under minimal
   127	assumptionsвҖ”sometimes turning worst-case sampling com-
   128	plexity upper bounds from exponential to polynomial (Guo
   129	et al., 2025). Nevertheless, important questions remain
   130	openвҖ”especially whether sampling can remain uniformly
   131	stable as the dimension increases, without incurring sub-
   132	stantially longer mixing times. This question has been the
   133	object of many studies for classical Langevin (Cotter et al.,
   134	2013; Durmus et al., 2017), but has not yet been rigorously
   135	addressed for ALD, except under strong regularity assump-
   136	tions on the target distribution (Cattiaux et al., 2025). Yet
   137	robustness is most critical in multimodal regimes, where
   138	classical Langevin can fail.

   271	Related Work
   272	Our work is primarily motivated by the recent wave
   273	of papers focusing on infinite-dimensional analyses for
   274	diffusion/score-based generative models (Kerrigan et al.,
   275	2023; Franzese et al., 2023; Baldassari et al., 2023; Pidstri-
   276	gach et al., 2024; Bond-Taylor & Willcocks, 2024; Baldas-
   277	sari et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2025; Hagemann et al., 2025;
   278	Baldassari et al., 2025; Franzese & Michiardi, 2025), and by
   279	the limitations of these results when the target distribution
   280	becomes multimodal.
   281	Non-asymptotic analyses of Langevin-type samplers are
   282	well developed under strong regularity assumptions (e.g.
   283	strong log-concavity), where one can obtain sharp guaran-
   284	tees on mixing and discretization error (Dalalyan, 2017;
   285	Durmus et al., 2017; 2019; Vempala & Wibisono, 2019;
   286	Chewi et al., 2025). These analyses also highlight a key lim-
   287	itation: for multimodal targets, comparable guarantees for
   288	classical Langevin are typically out of reach, since metasta-
   289	bility and energy barriers dominate the mixing behavior (Ma
   290	et al., 2019; Schlichting, 2019; Dong & Tong, 2022).
   291	Motivated by this difficulty, annealing-based strategies alter
   292	the sampling task through a prescribed path of intermedi-
   293	ate distributions that progressively flattens the landscape
   294	(Kirkpatrick et al., 1983; Gelfand & Mitter, 1990; Neal,
   295	2001). Evolving a Langevin diffusion along these sched-
   296	ules leads to annealed Langevin dynamics (Cattiaux et al.,
   297	2025). While early uses of ALD were largely heuristic
   298	(Song & Ermon, 2019; 2020), more recent theoryвҖ”driven

  1536	5.2. The Importance of Preconditioning
  1537	We now introduce error in both the initialization and the
  1538	score. To isolate the role in achieving dimension-uniformity
  1539	that our theory attributes to the preconditioner, we initialize
  1540	the ALD diffusion from a mixture with incorrect weights
  1541	(0.1, 0.9) but with the same component means and covari-
  1542	ances as the target, so that the initialization error does not
  1543	play a role in the stability analysis (the second sum on the
  1544	right-hand side of (12) is zero). The ALD drift, in con-
  1545	trast, is computed under a misspecified mixture in which the
  1546	component covariances are perturbed.
  1547	Specifically, in Figure 4 we consider an infinite-dimensional
  1548	bimodal Gaussian mixture target with weights (0.75, 0.25),
  1549	identical covariances ОЈ1 = ОЈ2 = Diag(jвҲ’2)jвүҘ1, and sep-
  1550	arated means m2 вҲ’m1 = 10e1. We study truncations at
  1551	dimensions d вҲҲ{1, 5, . . . , 75}. Following Section 4.2 and
  1552	the conditions of Appendix B.4, we introduce a covariance
  1553	perturbation вҲҶПғij whose spectrum decays as (jвҲ’3.5)jвүҘ1.
  1554	We then compare two preconditioners: one that satisfies
  1555	the conditions of Appendix B.4, О“ = Diag(jвҲ’3.5)jвүҘ1, and
  1556	one that violates them, О“ = Diag(jвҲ’1)jвүҘ1. The contrast
  1557	is once again evident: with the admissible preconditioner
  1558	the error remains uniformly controlled as d grows, whereas
  1559	with the non-admissible one it increases with dimensionвҖ”
  1560	highlighting that, under score error, preconditioning is cru-
  1561	cial for dimension-robust stability.
  1562	6. Discussion and Future Work
  1563	We studied the robustness of continuous-time annealed
  1564	Langevin dynamics with respect to dimension in a mul-
  1565	timodal setting. We showed that, within a well-defined
  1566	infinite-dimensional framework, appropriate design choices
  1567	yield dimension-uniform behavior, improving upon recent
  1568	bounds that degrade with dimension. Our analysis high-
  1569	lights the central role of preconditioningвҖ”a message that is
  1570	not surprising, and that connects our work to recent function-
  1571	space diffusion models literature (Pidstrigach et al., 2024;
  1572	Baldassari et al., 2025).
  1573	We derive explicit dimension-
  1574	dependent error bounds and identify conditions under which
  1575	they remain uniform, clarifying the interplay between the
  1576	mixture component means and covariances, the annealing
  1577	smoothing, and the preconditioner. As the authors of (Bal-
  1578	dassari et al., 2023; Pidstrigach et al., 2024; Baldassari et al.,
  1579	2025), we adopt simplifying assumptions, notably that the
  1580	mixture covariances are co-diagonalizable; this restriction
  1581	is less limiting here, since we allow infinitely many mix-
  1582	ture components, which still permit the approximation of
  1583	rich distributions. Several questions remain open, including
  1584	posterior sampling in Bayesian nonlinear inverse problems,
  1585	where classical Langevin is known to struggle (Bohr &
  1586	Nickl, 2024; Nickl, 2023). Developing an ALD analogue
  1587	of the theory in Baldassari et al. (2025) in this setting is a
  1588	natural next step, and one we are actively pursuing.

### Impact Assessment

**1. Technical Significance (70%):** 
The paper introduces a prompting technique, Structure of Thought (SoT), and a new benchmark, T2S-Bench, for text-to-structure reasoning. While explicitly extracting key nodes and links before generating an answer aligns intuitively with human reading strategies and yields measurable empirical improvements (+5.7% on Qwen2.5-7B), SoT is functionally a JSON-formatted Chain-of-Thought (CoT). Given the rapid evolution of intrinsic reasoning models (e.g., OpenAI o-series, DeepSeek-R1), static intermediate prompting strategies typically suffer from short half-lives in terms of practitioner adoption. On the benchmarking side, T2S-Bench is meticulously constructed with rigorous multi-stage human validation and spans diverse scientific domains. However, the NLP community is already saturated with reasoning and long-context benchmarks. While T2S-Bench provides a high-quality diagnostic tool for a specific bottleneck (node/link extraction), it does not introduce a fundamentally new capability or a foundational architectural shift. Furthermore, the downstream fine-tuning improvements are demonstrated primarily on smaller 7B/8B models, leaving its necessity and utility for state-of-the-art frontier models somewhat ambiguous.

**2. Scientific Significance (30%):** 
Scientifically, the paper confirms a well-established intuition: intermediate structural representations enhance complex text processing and aggregation. The finding that "structure extraction remains the bottleneck" (with models struggling significantly more on node identification than link prediction) is a valuable, albeit unsurprising, methodological observation. The work does not resolve a profound open theoretical question, nor does it reveal a critical flaw in existing paradigms. The intersection of text processing and explicit graph structuring has been extensively explored in the knowledge graph, information extraction, and neuro-symbolic literature. Consequently, this paper serves as a rigorous empirical validation of structuring for modern LLMs rather than the inception of a radically new scientific direction.

**3. The 3-Year Citation Projection:** 
Over the next three years, this paper is likely to accrue a moderate number of citations, primarily from comprehensive surveys on prompt engineering and by authors introducing subsequent reasoning benchmarks. However, it is highly unlikely to become a definitive, central benchmark that the field coalesces around, largely because LLM evaluation is rapidly shifting towards more dynamic, agentic, or open-ended tasks rather than static, template-based structural reasoning. I project roughly 30 to 50 citations, reflecting its status as a solid, well-executed, but ultimately incremental and niche contribution.

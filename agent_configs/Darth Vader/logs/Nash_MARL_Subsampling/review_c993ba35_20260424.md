# Comprehensive Review: Learning Approximate Nash Equilibria in Cooperative Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning via Mean-Field Subsampling

## Novelty
The paper tackles a highly relevant problem: cooperative Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) with one global agent and a large population of local agents, under strict communication constraints where the global agent can only observe $k \ll n$ local agents. The authors propose `ALTERNATING-MARL`, which uniquely combines subsampled mean-field Q-learning for the global agent and an episodic MDP reduction (solved via UCFH) for the local agents. While the individual components heavily rely on prior work (Anand et al. 2025; Dann et al. 2015), their synthesis within an alternating best-response framework and the clever technical reduction to chained micro-step MDPs represent a substantial methodological contribution.

## Technical Soundness
The theoretical framework is rigorous and meticulously constructed. The authors correctly identify the cooperative setting as an exact Markov Potential Game, ensuring the convergence of alternating best responses. The bounds on the approximation error—decaying at a rate of $\tilde{O}(1/\sqrt{k})$—are solid, properly leveraging total variation distance bounds and the Performance Difference Lemma. One minor concern is the lack of an explicit theoretical bound on the number of alternating steps $N_{\text{steps}}$ required for termination, although the $2\eta$ tolerance threshold guarantees finite-time convergence.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation validates the theoretical claims but suffers from significant gaps. The authors simulate a warehouse robotics environment with 1000 agents and thoroughly ablate the subsampling parameter $k$, demonstrating the fundamental tradeoff between approximation error and computational runtime. The statistical rigor (15 seeds, 50 rollouts) is commendable. However, the evaluation completely lacks external baselines. Comparing `ALTERNATING-MARL` against simpler heuristics or standard decentralized MARL algorithms (e.g., Independent Q-Learning) would have greatly strengthened the empirical argument for deploying such a complex framework.

## Impact
The work makes a strong scientific contribution to the theoretical foundations of MARL under partial observability. By establishing a framework that decouples sample complexity from the exponential joint action space, it provides valuable tools for researchers. However, the practical and technical significance is somewhat limited by the computational overhead of the alternating algorithm and the slow $O(1/\sqrt{k})$ error decay, which may restrict its adoption in real-world, latency-sensitive applications.

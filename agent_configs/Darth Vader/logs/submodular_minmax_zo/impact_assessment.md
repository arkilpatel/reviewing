### Impact Assessment
**1. Technical Significance (70%):**
The paper tackles a novel problem formulation: offline and online min-max optimization with submodular-concave objective functions. By utilizing the Lovász extension and a zeroth-order extragradient algorithm, the authors provide a solver that requires only function evaluations. However, the practical utility and feasibility of this specific method appear quite limited. The empirical validation is restricted to a highly sanitized toy problem: adversarial semi-supervised image segmentation on 50x50 pixel synthetic images. Comparing a custom zeroth-order optimizer on this toy task against a U-Net trained on 3000 samples of 50x50 images does not convincingly demonstrate real-world utility or scalability to modern, high-dimensional tasks. In practice, the adoption of this specific algorithm by the broader community is likely to be marginal because the empirical results do not prove it can handle the scale or complexity required by modern machine learning applications. The technical advance lies more in proving convergence theoretically rather than providing a tool that practitioners will immediately adopt.

**2. Scientific Significance (30%):**
The scientific contribution is sound but incremental. The paper successfully connects concepts from zeroth-order optimization, min-max games, and submodular set functions. By leveraging the Lovász extension to transform the discrete problem into a continuous convex-concave (though non-smooth) one, it establishes rigorous theoretical guarantees—namely an $O(\epsilon^{-2})$ sample complexity for the offline case and an $O(\sqrt{N \bar{P}_N})$ dynamic regret bound for the online setting. This advances our understanding of how to theoretically analyze and solve robust submodular optimization problems under adversarial conditions. However, the theoretical results are largely a systematic application of existing zeroth-order extragradient analysis to the Lovász extension, rather than a fundamental paradigm shift, the resolution of a major open theoretical question, or the revelation of a critical failure mode.

**3. The 3-Year Citation Projection:**
Over the next three years, this paper is likely to accrue a modest number of citations (perhaps 10 to 30). These citations will predominantly come from the theoretical optimization community, particularly researchers working on submodular optimization and min-max games, citing it as prior work for robust or online combinatorial problems. It is highly unlikely to be cited by applied researchers or industry practitioners due to the toy-scale nature of the experiments and the lack of a highly scalable, ready-to-deploy solver. 

**Impact Score: 3.5 / 10**

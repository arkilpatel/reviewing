# Final Review: Curiosity is Knowledge: Self-Consistent Learning and No-Regret Optimization with Active Inference

### Novelty
The core methodological proposal of the paper—the Active Inference (AIF) acquisition function defined as $\alpha(x|D_t) = \beta_t I(s; (x,y)|D_t) - \mathbb{E}[h_t(y)]$—is completely derivative. The authors themselves explicitly acknowledge in Section 3 that this exact acquisition rule was previously introduced by Li et al. (2026) under the name "pragmatic curiosity." Consequently, the paper's sole claimed contribution is the theoretical analysis of an existing method. Unfortunately, the theoretical delta is practically non-existent. The proofs for both Theorem 5.1 and Theorem 6.1 rely on mathematically trivial, five-line algebraic manipulations. By defining a "sufficient curiosity" condition that merely forces the maximum of the acquisition function to be non-negative, the authors trivially conclude that $\mathbb{E}[h_t(x_t)] \le \beta_t I(x_t)$. Summing this inequality over time circumvents the deep, non-trivial analysis typical of foundational Bayesian Optimization (BO) theory (e.g., Srinivas et al., 2009) and offers absolutely no new analytical tools or insights to the field.

### Technical Soundness
The theoretical claims of self-consistent learning and no-regret optimization are fundamentally flawed and practically vacuous. Both Theorem 5.1 and 6.1 heavily rely on the "sufficient curiosity" condition (Assumption iii), which mathematically destroys the validity of the "no-regret" claim. As learning progresses and the agent's posterior concentrates, the expected information gain $I(s; (x, y) | D_{t-1})$ approaches 0. For the ratio $\beta_t$ to remain bounded, the expected heuristic regret must converge to 0 faster than the denominator. However, the expected regret at the optimal point scales with the confidence interval ($\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{I})$). Thus, the required $\beta_t$ evaluates to $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{I})$, which diverges to infinity as $I \to 0$. Because the cumulative regret bound in Eq. (9) scales multiplicatively with $\bar{\beta}_T = \max_{t \le T} \beta_t$, the resulting regret bound is super-linear, rendering the "no-regret" guarantee mathematically meaningless. 

Furthermore, Theorem 6.1 suffers from a circular regret bound via its "heuristic alignment" assumption. The bound incorporates a cumulative misalignment term dependent on $\max_{y} |r(y) - h_t(y)|$. To make this term sublinear, the heuristic $h_t$ must accurately approximate the true instantaneous regret $r(y) = f(x^*) - y$. Implicitly requiring a uniform approximation of the true regret requires knowledge of the global optimum $f(x^*)$—the exact problem BO aims to solve! Pushing the optimization difficulty into an assumed small $B_t$ makes the bound circular and utterly useless. This disconnect between theory and practice is further exacerbated by the experiments, which simply tune $\beta$ as a constant hyperparameter, completely ignoring the theoretical mandate that $\beta_t$ must diverge to infinity.

### Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation is fundamentally broken due to the complete absence of any baseline comparisons. The authors test the AIF acquisition function on complex domains (2D plume fields and 40D power grids) but only perform internal ablations of their own method, sweeping the curiosity coefficient $\beta$ and different heuristic functions. To claim that a method successfully unifies BO and BED, or is an effective optimization algorithm, it is mandatory to compare it against established algorithms like GP-UCB, Expected Improvement (EI), or Entropy Search. Without benchmarking against these standard approaches, it is impossible to evaluate whether the proposed AIF method is competitive or practically viable. Furthermore, the experiments are averaged over a mere 5 random seeds—far too few to draw robust conclusions in BO, which is notoriously sensitive to initial evaluations. There is no meaningful error analysis beyond artificially injected heuristic bias.

### Impact
The technical and scientific significance of this paper is extremely low. The AIF acquisition function is borrowed from prior work, and the theoretical guarantees intended to elevate it are mathematically circular and vacuous. Practitioners will not adopt a method whose theoretical backing requires impossible oracle knowledge and a diverging hyperparameter, especially when the empirical study provides zero evidence of superiority—or even parity—with established BO methods. Theoretical researchers will immediately identify the circularity of the regret bounds and the triviality of the proofs. Ultimately, this paper fails to advance our fundamental understanding of exploration-exploitation or provide a reliable tool for practitioners.

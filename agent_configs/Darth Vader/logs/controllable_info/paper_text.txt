Controllable Information Production
Anonymous Authors1
Abstract
Intrinsic Motivation (IM) is a paradigm for gen-
erating intelligent behavior without external util-
ities. The existing information-theoretic meth-
ods for IM are predominantly based on informa-
tion transmission, which explicitly depends on the
designer’s choice of which random variables en-
gage in transmission. In this work, we introduce
a novel IM principle, Controllable Information
Production (CIP), that avoids both external utili-
ties and designer-specified variables. We derive
the CIP objective from Optimal Control, show-
ing a connection between extrinsic and intrin-
sic behaviors. CIP appears as the gap between
open-loop and closed-loop Kolmogorov–Sinai en-
tropies, which simultaneously rewards the pur-
suit and regulation of chaos. We establish key
theoretical properties of CIP and demonstrate its
effectiveness on standard IM benchmarks.
1. Introduction
Intrinsic Motivation (IM) is a biologically inspired paradigm
for generating intelligent behavior without externally speci-
fied goals. A range of plausible information-theoretic objec-
tives such as Empowerment (Klyubin et al., 2005), Curios-
ity (Schmidhuber, 2010), Information Gain (Bhavya et al.,
2025), Diversity (DIAYN) (Eysenbach et al., 2019), and
Predictive Information have been proposed for IM and suc-
cessfully applied to control problems including stabilization
(Tiomkin et al., 2024), object manipulation, locomotion
(Karl et al., 2022), and transportation. Despite these suc-
cesses, several fundamental gaps remain.
First, existing IM methods are predominantly based on
mutual information, which implicitly assumes information
transmission and requires designers to explicitly specify
which random variables (RV) participate in information
exchange (Shannon, 1948). This introduces external knowl-

Intuitively, Controllable Information Production (CIP) re-
wards an agent for seeking out and suppressing controllable
sources of chaos. The objective compares the rate at which
the environment produces entropy when actions are injected
blindly (open-loop) versus when they are selected adap-
tively in response to state feedback (closed-loop). A large
gap indicates regimes where the environment rapidly pro-
duces entropy yet remains sensitive to control: open-loop
behavior generates high entropy, while closed-loop actions
suppress it. Hence, the agent is motivated to position itself
near the edge of chaos, where small interventions have max-
imal impact on system evolution (Tower, 2024; Zhang et al.,
2025; Bertschinger et al., 2004). Crucially, purely chaotic
or uncontrollable dynamics are not rewarded: when entropy
cannot be influenced by the agent’s actions, open-loop and
closed-loop entropy production coincide, yielding low CIP.
As such, the objective values chaos that can be consumed
through control.

Q1. How is information transmitted through dynamics?
Q2. How is information produced by dynamics?
Existing IM methods based on Shannon mutual information
respond to Q1. They assume information already exists
somewhere (e.g., in actions, messages, or skills) and that
dynamics propagate it to future observations or states. This
requires choosing which variables are deemed important
sources of information and which are relevant targets for
transmission. As such, the resulting IM agent is endowed
with a designer-specified information interface, in which the
choice of source and target variables implicitly constrains
how the agent perceives, acts, and exchanges information
with the environment.
CIP responds to Q2, which involves no designer choice but
rather captures an intrinsic property of dynamics with only
two possible modes of interaction between agent and envi-
ronment: open-loop or closed-loop. Q2 does not privilege
any variables as sources or targets of information. Instead,
it has a clear interpretation grounded solely in dynamical
systems properties and, as we show, emerges naturally from
OC theory.
Our main contributions are as follows:
1. We formulate a new approach to IM as a problem of
information production rather than transmission, which
emerges from OC theory. This closes the knowledge
gap in understanding the connection between IM and
OC, and opens doors for novel optimization schemes
grounded in OC.
2. We establish key theoretical properties of CIP includ-
ing its positiveness and its connection to the Riccati
Equation: the fundamental equation in OC. The latter
provides a new method for estimating KSE through
Riccati Recursions.
In this paper we focus on a new conceptual paradigm in IM
and its theoretical properties, deferring the development of
efficient optimization methods for large scale experiment
to future work. We demonstrate the effectiveness of CIP
on important benchmark problems in IM, including chaotic
dynamics, while experiments with arbitrary large scale envi-
ronments are beyond scope of the current work, and as such
deferred to follow up work. Code to reproduce experiments

2. Existing Intrinsic Motivation Methods
A wide variety of IM objectives have been proposed to
generate intelligent behavior without external goals. These
methods can be categorized based on which random vari-
ables (RVs) participate in information exchange. For ex-
ample, Empowerment (Klyubin et al., 2005; Salge et al.,
2014) computes the channel capacity from an open-loop
control sequence to the final state, effectively measuring an
agent’s controllability. Curiosity-based IM methods such as
Active Inference (Parr et al., 2022; Friston, 2010) and Sur-
prise Maximization (Schmidhuber, 2010; Burda et al., 2019)
measure the information gained by the agent’s model param-
eters upon observing states. Diversity-based methods such
as DIAYN (Eysenbach et al., 2019) measure information
between latent skill representations and states.
By selecting which variables transmit and receive infor-
mation, an IM objective can be obtained that encodes an
implicit bias about what the agent “should” consider to be
important or privileged. In (Tiomkin et al., 2024), it is
shown that an entire family of IM objectives can be derived
from a single Proto Objective through different designer
choices regarding which input RVs are “important” (equiv-
alently, the information source) and which relevance RVs
allow reconstruction of the input RVs after transmission
through dynamics. This variety of IM objectives empha-
sizes the designer choice and subsequent bias in the resulting
methods.
IM methods have been shown to be effective in optimal
control problems which otherwise require engineered re-
ward in order to solve. For example, the challenging task
of swinging up and balancing the chaotic double pendu-
lum was shown to be solved with Empowerment (Tiomkin
et al., 2024). Other work shows that an Active Inference
approach leads to unsupervised object manipulation (Fer-
raro et al., 2025). Diversity-based methods such as DIAYN
have been used to learn a repertoire of reusable skills that
can be composed or fine-tuned to solve downstream control
tasks, effectively replacing hand-designed reward shaping
with unsupervised skill discovery (Eysenbach et al., 2019).
While such results demonstrate the practical use of IM for
control, the objectives themselves are typically introduced
independently of OC principles. As a result, the relationship
between existing IM, OC, and dynamical systems theory
remains unclear, which hinders the interpretation and limits
applicability of IM methods in many domains.
3. Overview of Kolmogorov-Sinai Entropy
Entropy production plays a fundamental role in the analy-
sis of dynamical systems and control. Kolmogorov-Sinai
entropy (KSE) quantifies in bit/sec how rapidly a dynami-
cal system generates new, distinguishable trajectories over
time (Kolmogorov, 1958; Sinai, 1959; Walters, 1982; Pe-
tersen, 1983). Given a finite partition of the state space,
observing the system over multiple steps refines this parti-
tion according to the dynamics; the average growth rate of
the resulting uncertainty defines an entropy rate. KS entropy
is the maximum such rate over all finite partitions, yielding

information criteria on the dynamics, CIP emerges in terms
of KSE, a quantity native to dynamical systems. As a result,
CIP does not require specifying sender or receiver vari-
ables for information transmission and instead captures the
intrinsic gap between open-loop and closed-loop entropy
production as a fundamental consequence of control.

4. The CIP Principle
The edge of chaos phenomenon suggests that intelligence
arises in regimes where the environment is chaotic yet still
sensitive to control. Achieving this requires the agent to
solve two problems: first, it must identify sources of chaos
by generating trajectories with high open-loop entropy pro-
duction; second, it must determine whether this chaos is
capable of being regulated by reducing its closed-loop en-
tropy. The difference between the entropy that exists versus
the entropy that remains under OC is precisely what CIP
measures. We now derive this objective from OC principles
and establish its key theoretical properties.

5. Experiments
We conduct a series of experiments to evaluate the effec-
tiveness of CIP-based control across dynamical systems
of increasing complexity. Our primary goal is to under-
stand how a CIP-based agent behaves in environments with
varying degrees of chaos, and whether a controller that
maximizes CIP can reliably steer the system toward states
that are both highly informative (high open-loop entropy)
and controllable (low closed-loop entropy). In particular,
we investigate whether CIP naturally induces exploratory
behavior in low-chaos regimes and stabilization in highly
sensitive regions of the state space.
We evaluate our approach on three classical benchmark
systems that span a range of dynamical complexity:
• Single Pendulum: A low-dimensional system with
one degree of freedom, used to demonstrate basic en-
ergy accumulation, swing-up, and stabilization.
• Cart Pole: A system with indirect actuation, where
control is applied through horizontal cart motion rather
than direct torque on the pendulum, introducing inter-
mediate complexity and delayed effects.
• Double Pendulum: A two-degree-of-freedom system
with strongly chaotic dynamics, serving as a challeng-
ing testbed for evaluating whether CIP can guide both
exploration and fine-grained stabilization in highly sen-
sitive regimes.
To isolate the role of CIP as an IM, we deliberately adopt
a simple controller architecture. Specifically, we use a
random-sampling-based model predictive control (MPC)

The overall control procedure is summarized in Algorithm
1. The agent maintains a search distribution D over action
sequences and updates it through online interaction to max-

using the Improved Cross-Entropy Method (iCEM) (Pinneri

tasks. At each planning step, iCEM samples candidate ac-

iteratively refines the sampling distribution by fitting an
isotropic Gaussian to the top-performing (elite) sequences.
This mechanism allows the controller to balance exploration
of novel behaviors with exploitation of previously successful
action patterns.

5.2. Behavior in Benchmark Environments
The behavior of the CIP-based controller on the single pen-
dulum task is shown in Figure 2a. The agent rapidly accumu-
lates energy through coordinated oscillations and stabilizes
the pendulum in the upright position. The corresponding
CIP values are shown in Figure 2d. Notably, CIP increases
before the pendulum reaches the upright configuration. This
occurs because CIP is computed over a future planned tra-
jectory: although the current state may not be highly chaotic,
the planned sequence of actions can lead to trajectories with
high information production. As expected, the information
production rate of the single pendulum is lower than that of
the double pendulum, reflecting its comparatively simple
dynamics.
We next evaluate CIP on the cart-pole swing-up and balanc-
ing task in Figure 2b. Unlike the pendulum systems, the cart
pole lacks direct torque-based actuation at the pendulum
joint. The controller must therefore discover a coordinated
oscillatory strategy that moves the cart to indirectly inject
energy into the pendulum. The CIP-based controller suc-
cessfully identifies this strategy and achieves stable balanc-
ing. The corresponding CIP values in Figure 2d lie between
those of the single and double pendulum, consistent with the
intermediate dynamical complexity of the cart-pole system.
Finally, we consider the double pendulum, a system well
known for its extreme sensitivity to initial conditions and
chaotic behavior. Despite this challenge, the CIP-based con-
troller is able to swing up and indefinitely stabilize the sys-
tem near the upright configuration. This behavior highlights
the controller’s ability to apply strong, energy-injecting ac-
tions in low-chaos regions (near the bottom) and transition
to fine-grained, stabilizing control in highly chaotic regimes

6. Discussion
This work introduces Controllable Information Production
(CIP), a principled IM for continuous control. Unlike most
existing IM formulations, which are postulated from heuris-
tics or biological inspiration, CIP emerges directly from
OC theory. It quantifies the controllable chaos produced
in an environment. We observe that CIP gives rise to be-
haviors that closely align with prior intuitions about how
intrinsically motivated agents should act when deployed in
a dynamical control system. Using a deliberately simple
MPC-based controller, we show that maximizing CIP con-
sistently drives agents toward states at the edge of chaos
across dynamical systems of increasing complexity.
6.1. Interpretation of CIP
CIP naturally decomposes into two complementary incen-
tives. The first component, open-loop entropy production,
captures the tendency of the uncontrolled system to increase
uncertainty. Maximizing this term drives the agent toward
regions of the state space with chaotic dynamics, analogous
to exploratory behavior. Importantly, this quantity is blind
to feedback and thus reflects the environment’s intrinsic
capacity for information generation.
The second component, closed-loop entropy production,
measures the residual chaos that remains once feedback
control is applied. Minimizing this term encourages the
agent to actively suppress uncertainty through its actions,
favoring regions of the dynamics where chaos is not merely
present but controllable, analogous to exploitation. Defined
as the difference between these two quantities, CIP therefore
measures precisely how much of the system’s information
production is amenable to control.
Taken together, CIP formalizes the notion of controllable
chaos. It rewards agents for finding pockets of controlla-
bility within the chaos, where the dynamics are rich and
highly highly sensitive to control, unifying exploration and
exploitation within a single objective.
6.2. Emergence of CIP from Optimal Control
To the best of our knowledge, CIP is the first IM derived di-
rectly from the structure of optimal control. It arises from a
decomposition of the Hessian of the optimal value function
into open- and closed-loop entropy production rates, which
we show are equivalent to the KSE of the corresponding
dynamics. In light of this principled derivation, the result-
ing behavior closely aligns with long-standing intuitions in
intrinsic motivation: an agent should seek states in which it
has maximal influence over future outcomes.
Importantly, although CIP is derived using the machin-
ery of optimal control, it remains entirely goal-agnostic.
Within OC, the optimal feedback policy naturally decom-
poses into extrinsic and intrinsic components, where CIP
emerges solely from the intrinsic part, specifically from the
second-order structure of the value function. This reveals a
direct and previously unexplored connection between value
curvature and closed-loop information production. In par-
ticular, dynamics which are well-controlled correspond to
low closed-loop KSE and therefore to a Hessian which
converges, whereas uncontrolled dynamics lead to larger
closed-loop KSE, indicating that DARE does not converge.
Our theoretical results further indicate that intrinsic motiva-
tion should be framed as a rate of information production
(e.g., nats per second), rather than as an absolute information
measure. This perspective aligns more naturally with dy-
namical systems theory and resolves ambiguities associated
with variable-length trajectories.
6.3. Information Transmission and Production
A key conceptual distinction between CIP and most exist-
ing IM methods lies in the difference between information
transmission and information production. Prior approaches
typically ask questions such as, “How much do my actions
tell me about my future states?” These formulations require
the designer to specify specific random variables such as
actions, states, or latent representations between which in-
formation is transmitted. Such choices inevitably introduce
design bias.
CIP, in contrast, asks a more fundamental question: “How
much chaos would the system generate in the absence of
feedback, and how much of that chaos can my control sup-

6.4. Behavior Generated By a CIP-Based Controller
CIP is particularly well suited to trajectory-based planning
methods such as MPC, as it is defined along system tra-
jectories and can be efficiently computed via Riccati-like
recursions. Even when implemented with a simple random-
shooting MPC controller, maximizing CIP produces deliber-
ate, interpretable, and non-trivial behavior across all tested
environments.
Empirically, the agent seeks out highly chaotic regions of
the state space and then actively stabilizes itself within them.
This behavior is particularly impressive in the chaotic dou-
ble pendulum, where the agent consistently reaches and
maintains configurations that are both highly sensitive and
controllable. The resulting policy demonstrate an awareness
of where chaos arises in the environment and how it can
be regulated through feedback, despite the absence of any
task-specific reward.
These observations suggest that CIP captures a fundamental
aspect of intelligent behavior: the drive to place oneself in
situations that are maximally informative yet amenable to
control.
6.5. Limitations and Future Work
One limitation of the current implementation lies in the nu-
merical calculation of open-loop entropy. Our approach
involves backward recursions which can develop extremely
large values in the absence of stabilizing feedback. This
issue is most pronounced for open-loop entropy and can
lead to numerical instability in systems with very high in-
formation production rates. Future work will focus on de-
veloping more stable computational schemes for estimating
open-loop entropy, potentially through regularization or al-
ternative formulations of the recursion.
A second limitation is the simplicity of the controller used
to instantiate CIP. While random-shooting MPC with iCEM
suffices to demonstrate the principle, we found that it does
not scale well to high-dimensional or long-horizon tasks.
An important direction for future work is to integrate CIP
into learning-based controllers, such as policy gradient or
actor–critic methods.
Finally, an exciting avenue for future research is the inte-
gration of CIP with extrinsic objectives. Since CIP emerges
naturally as the intrinsic component of optimal control, it
provides a principled foundation for studying how intrinsic
and extrinsic motivations interact. We believe this combina-
tion has great potential to improve performance in a wide
range of continuous control problems.
Impact Statement
This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for IM
in continuous environments. The development of theoreti-
cally grounded methods for IM has the potential to improve
robot learning by reducing reliance on human-designed re-
ward functions, which are often difficult to engineer.
Agents driven by IM may exhibit unpredictable behavior due
to the lack of explicit human guidance. In real-world robotic
systems, such behavior could pose risks to human safety if
deployed without appropriate oversight or safeguards.
As a primarily theoretical contribution evaluated in simu-
lated environments, the immediate societal impact of this
work is limited. Any real-world effects would depend on
downstream applications, integration with safety mecha-
nisms, and deployment decisions made by practitioners.

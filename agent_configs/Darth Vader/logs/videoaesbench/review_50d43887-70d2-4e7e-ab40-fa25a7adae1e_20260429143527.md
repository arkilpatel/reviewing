## Overall Assessment

This paper introduces VideoAesBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate the video aesthetics perception capabilities of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). While constructing such a benchmark is a useful engineering effort, the paper suffers from significant flaws across novelty, technical soundness, experimental rigor, and anticipated impact.

### Novelty
### Claimed Contributions
1. The introduction of VideoAesBench, which the authors claim is the first comprehensive benchmark designed specifically to evaluate the video aesthetics perception capabilities of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs).
2. The benchmark features diverse video content (UGC, AIGC, RGC, compression, gaming), multiple question formats (including the introduction of multiple-choice questions), and covers 12 fine-grained aesthetic dimensions categorized into visual form, visual style, and visual affectiveness.
3. A comprehensive benchmarking of 23 state-of-the-art open-source and closed-source LMMs, providing empirical findings on their current capabilities and limitations in video aesthetics understanding.

### Prior Work Assessment
- **Benchmark Construction Framework**: The paper closely follows the blueprint established by recent LMM quality and aesthetics benchmarks, most notably AesBench (for image aesthetics) and Q-Bench-Video (for video perceptual quality). Applying the evaluation paradigm of AesBench to the video domain is a logical, highly predictable next step. The dataset creation methodology—sourcing existing videos, collecting human descriptions, and using LLMs (Gemini/GPT) to synthesize QA pairs—is entirely standard and does not introduce any methodological novelty. The addition of "multiple-choice" as a novel question format is trivial. The delta is **Incremental**.
- **Aesthetic Dimensions Taxonomy**: The 12 aesthetic dimensions are largely derived from traditional film and photography aesthetics. While adapted for video (incorporating aspects like "shot size" and "pacing" under viewer interest), it is a straightforward translation of existing aesthetic theories into an LMM evaluation framework, very similar to the 8 dimensions in AesBench. The delta is **Incremental**.
- **Empirical Insights**: The findings derived from the benchmarking experiments are superficial and expected. Conclusions such as "closed-source models generally outperform open-source models," "multiple-choice questions are harder than True/False," and "performance is unbalanced across dimensions" offer no new fundamental understanding of *why* LMMs process aesthetics the way they do or how to structurally improve them. This is a classic case of reporting leaderboard numbers without deeper insight. The delta is **Minimal**.

### Novelty Verdict
Incremental

### Justification
This paper represents a predictable "domain transfer" contribution, extending the concept of LMM image aesthetics evaluation (AesBench) to the video modality. While creating a benchmark requires engineering effort, the conceptual and methodological novelty is very low. The paper employs a standard human-LLM collaboration pipeline to generate a relatively small dataset (1,804 QA pairs). Furthermore, the empirical findings fail to reveal any surprising, non-obvious properties or mechanisms of LMMs, instead reporting generic performance trends that could be easily anticipated by anyone familiar with current multimodal models. The work is a useful but highly incremental resource for the community that disguises domain transfer as a major contribution.

### Missing References
The paper adequately cites the most directly related prior work, including AesBench and Q-Bench-Video, so there are no egregious missing references. However, it fails to clearly articulate why this video extension provides substantially new insights beyond what is already known from image aesthetics benchmarking.

### Claims Inventory

### Verification Results

2. **Error Found**: There is an internal contradiction regarding the total number of videos.
3. **Verified**: The 4 types of questions are evaluated and structurally verified in the methodology.
4. **Verified**: The 12 dimensions are explicitly utilized in the experiments.
5. **Error Found**: There is an arithmetic discrepancy regarding the number of evaluated open-source models stated in the text versus the tables.

8. **Verified**: Variance in performance across dimensions and video sources is consistently shown in the result tables.

### Errors and Concerns
1. **Invalid Random Guess Baseline for Open-Ended Questions (Significant Error)**: Table 3 reports a "Random guess" baseline score of 33.59% for open-ended (OE) questions. According to Section A.1, open-ended responses are evaluated by an LLM (GPT-5.2) using a strict 0-2 scale, where an irrelevant, nonsensical, or empty response explicitly receives a score of 0. A true random guess (random text or no text) would consistently score 0. It is practically impossible to achieve a 33.59% average accuracy through random guessing on this generative task. This suggests a major flaw in the baseline evaluation script. Furthermore, the random guess baseline for multiple-choice (MC) questions is omitted entirely ("-"), yet an "Overall" baseline score of 33.26% is somehow computed and presented.

2. **Contradictory Dataset Statistics (Significant Error)**: The Abstract and Section 1 explicitly state that the benchmark contains "1,804 videos". Additionally, the "Sampled Size" column in Table 2 lists the number of videos per source, which sums exactly to 1,804. However, Table 1 states that there are "1,641" Source Videos and 1,804 Q&A Pairs. This is a direct internal contradiction. If there are 1,641 videos and 1,804 Q&A pairs, then the repeated claim of having 1,804 unique videos is factually incorrect.

3. **Model Count Contradiction (Minor Error)**: Section 4.1 explicitly states: "we select 16 open-source models and 5 closed-source models". However, the very same paragraph proceeds to list 18 open-source models, and Table 3 subsequently includes results for exactly 18 open-source models (making the total 23, rather than 21).

4. **Citation and Typographical Errors (Minor Error)**:
   - In Section 4.1, the closed-source model GPT-5.2 is listed with an empty citation: "GPT-5.2 ()".
   - In Tables 4 and 5, the model "o3" is incorrectly cited as "(Singh et al., 2025)", which corresponds to the GPT-5.2 paper. In Table 3, it is correctly cited as "(OpenAI, 2025)".
   - The paper interchangeably refers to the evaluator LLM as "GPT-5" and "GPT-5.2" across Section 3.4 and 4.1.

### Internal Consistency Check
- **Dataset Size**: Major contradiction between Table 1 (1,641 source videos) and the rest of the text/tables (1,804 videos).
- **Model Counts**: Contradiction between Section 4.1's claim of "16 open-source models" and the 18 models actually evaluated.
- **Scoring Consistency**: Calculating the overall score for GPT-5.2 based on the reported subset scores and data distribution yields ~63.29%, while the table reports 63.33%. This is a minor discrepancy that likely stems from rounding issues but demonstrates general consistency.

Not applicable. The paper introduces a dataset and an empirical benchmark; there are no formal theoretical guarantees or mathematical proofs to assess against experimental conditions.

### Experimental Rigor
### Claims-to-Experiments Mapping
- **Claim 1:** VideoAesBench is a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating LMMs' understanding of video aesthetics. Supported by an experiment benchmarking 23 LMMs across various video types, aesthetic dimensions, and question formats.
- **Claim 2:** Current LMMs only contain "basic" video aesthetics perception ability and their performance remains "incomplete and imprecise." Supported by the overall accuracy results where the best models achieve ~67%. However, this claim is not fully supported because there is no human baseline to establish what a "good" or "expert" score should be on these specific questions.

### Baseline Assessment
- **Human Baseline (Missing):** For a highly subjective task like video aesthetics assessment, a human performance baseline or inter-annotator agreement metric is mandatory. The paper provides none, making it impossible to know if 67% accuracy is poor, average, or superhuman.
- **Task-Specific Baselines (Missing):** The evaluation is restricted to general-purpose LMMs. It does not compare them against state-of-the-art, specialized video aesthetic or quality assessment models.
- **LMM Baselines:** The selection of 23 LMMs (including state-of-the-art models like Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-5.2, o3, and Qwen3-VL) is comprehensive and provides a strong representation of the current landscape.

### Dataset Assessment
- **Dataset Size:** The dataset is extremely small for a modern benchmark, containing only 1,804 questions. Related benchmarks like Q-Bench (2,990) and AesBench (8,400) are much larger. 
- **Diversity:** The dataset effectively includes diverse sources (UGC, AIGC, RGC, Compression, and Gaming).
- **Contamination and Bias:** The pipeline uses Gemini-2.5 and GPT-5.2 to generate the ground-truth captions and questions. Although human annotators checked them, relying so heavily on LLMs to generate ground-truth data for an LLM benchmark risks injecting LLM-specific biases into the dataset.

### Metric Assessment
- **Closed-ended Metrics:** Exact match accuracy is standard and appropriate for multiple-choice, single-choice, and true/false questions.
- **Open-ended Metrics (Flawed):** The paper uses GPT-5 as an LLM-as-a-judge to evaluate open-ended responses on a 0-2 scale. This has two major red flags:
  1. There is no correlation analysis with human judgments to validate this metric.
  2. **Severe self-preference bias:** In Table 3, GPT-5.2 scores 69.20% on Open-Ended (OE) questions, beating the next best model (o3) by over 7 points and Claude-Sonnet-4.5 by 10 points. Yet, on the objective closed-ended questions, Claude and Qwen3-VL consistently outperform GPT-5.2. This heavily implies the GPT-5 evaluator is biased toward GPT-5.2's outputs, invalidating the open-ended results.

### Statistical Rigor
- **Variance and Runs:** The paper does not report any variance, standard deviations, confidence intervals, or multiple seeds for generation.
- **Significance:** There are no statistical significance tests. 
- **Robustness:** With only 1,804 questions, breaking results down into small sub-categories (e.g., 86 compression videos or 84 game videos) without confidence intervals means the fine-grained findings in Table 5 are likely noisy and not statistically reliable.

### Ablation Assessment
- Traditional component ablations are not applicable for a benchmark paper. The authors do break down performance across video sources, aesthetic dimensions, and question types to analyze model capabilities. However, there is no ablation of their dataset creation pipeline (e.g., showing the impact of the human refinement step versus pure LLM generation).

### Missing Experiments
- **Human Evaluation / Inter-Annotator Agreement:** Absolutely essential for establishing the upper bound of a subjective benchmark.
- **Human Correlation for LLM-as-a-Judge:** Validating the GPT-5 open-ended evaluator against human scoring.
- **Comparison with Specialized Models:** Evaluating traditional video aesthetic scoring models.
- **Statistical Significance Testing:** To confirm if the small differences between top models are meaningful given the small dataset size.

### Error Analysis Assessment
- **Fundamentally Lacking:** The paper contains absolutely no qualitative error analysis. Despite claiming LMMs are "incomplete and imprecise," the authors do not provide any visual examples, case studies, or failure mode categorizations showing *where* or *why* the models fail. 

### Overall Experimental Rigor Verdict
Significant gaps. The benchmark lacks a human upper-bound for a subjective task, rendering absolute numbers uninterpretable. The open-ended evaluation uses an unvalidated LLM-as-a-judge that shows glaring self-preference bias. Furthermore, the small dataset size combined with a complete lack of statistical testing and qualitative error analysis limits the reliability and insights of the benchmark.

### Significance & Impact
### Impact Assessment

**1. Technical Significance (70%):** 
VideoAesBench addresses the evaluation of video aesthetics perception in Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). While evaluating aesthetics is practically relevant—especially with the continuous rise of AI-generated content (AIGC) and user-generated content (UGC) platforms—the technical significance of this specific benchmark is moderate. The evaluation space for LMMs is highly saturated with massive, generalized video understanding benchmarks (e.g., Video-MME, MVBench). Although VideoAesBench carves out a specialized niche, its relatively small scale (1,804 question-answer pairs) and narrow focus suggest it will see adoption primarily within the specialized subfield of Video Quality Assessment (VQA) and video generation evaluation, rather than becoming a primary evaluation hurdle for general foundation model development. It provides a useful and well-structured testbed but does not introduce a disruptive tool, a fundamentally new capability, or a paradigm shift in how models are built or evaluated.

**2. Scientific Significance (30%):** 
The scientific impact of this work is mostly limited to incremental empirical observations. The paper concludes that current leading LMMs (such as Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Qwen3-VL) only possess basic video aesthetics perception abilities and still struggle with more complex question formats like multiple-choice. This finding is largely expected and directly mirrors existing conclusions from image aesthetics (e.g., AesBench) and low-level vision evaluations (e.g., Q-Bench). The paper does not reveal a critical methodological failure mode, settle a scientific debate, or introduce a novel theoretical framework. Instead, it methodically applies standard benchmark construction practices (dataset aggregation, human-in-the-loop QA generation, and LLM-as-a-judge for open-ended questions) to a new domain.

**3. The 3-Year Citation Projection:** 
Over the next three years, this paper will likely secure a solid but bounded number of citations. It will primarily be cited by researchers developing specialized video quality assessment LMMs, those building reward models to align video generation models, or review papers surveying the landscape of multimodal capabilities. However, because it is an incremental extension of existing aesthetics assessment methodologies to the video modality, it lacks the broad, cross-domain utility required to become a heavily cited, foundational benchmark that the entire AI community coalesces around.

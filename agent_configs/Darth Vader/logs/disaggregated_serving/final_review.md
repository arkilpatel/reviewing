### Novelty
The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem: serving multi-round LLM workflows under disaggregated prefill-decode architectures. The growing importance of agentic workflows makes this a timely area of research. However, the proposed solutions—runtime adaptive scheduling, prefill reordering, and an offline deployment planner—are fundamentally incremental. They rely on straightforward applications of existing, classical distributed systems techniques rather than introducing new architectural paradigms. The adaptive routing mechanism is a standard threshold-based load-balancing heuristic ported to the context of incremental prefill tasks. Similarly, the prefill reordering algorithm is a basic look-ahead window approach for deadline scheduling, reminiscent of classical operations research algorithms. The Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation for deployment planning is an adaptation of existing search strategies seen in systems like AlpaServe. While the paper correctly identifies a novel operational context (multi-round disaggregated serving), the delta over prior work in terms of system design is minimal. Furthermore, while the paper cites important prior works on prefill-decode disaggregation such as DistServe, Mooncake, and Splitwise, it lacks a rigorous contrast of its adaptive routing decisions against the mechanisms those specific systems employ. Overall, the contribution lies primarily in assembling these existing techniques for a specific use case, offering no fundamentally new theoretical insights or mechanisms.

### Technical Soundness
The paper's technical foundation is hampered by critical errors, particularly concerning the offline deployment planner. Equation (5) formulates an objective to minimize a surrogate variable $Z$, subject to $Z \ge \tau_{pre}(n)$ and $Z \ge \tau_{dec}(n)$ for any deployed configuration, where $\tau$ represents the simulated P95 latency. A critical flaw emerges because P95 latency in any queuing system is inherently non-linear and highly sensitive to the arrival rate per replica. The text claims the MILP solver "accepts the simulated P95 latency coefficients ... as constant parameters." If these are constant, the formulation fails to account for the reduced load per replica as the solver increases the data parallelism degree, thereby removing any mathematical incentive for the solver to allocate more than one replica and rendering the optimization meaningless. Conversely, if the simulator dynamically evaluates the full configuration (as suggested contradictorily in Appendix A.1), the problem becomes non-linear and cannot be straightforwardly solved by an MILP solver. Consequently, the claim of guaranteeing a "global optimum" is invalid as the mathematical formulation is structurally misaligned with real queuing dynamics.

A secondary concern arises in the Adaptive Routing Cost Function (Algorithm 1). During heavy system load where both TTFT and ITL slack thresholds are exceeded, the algorithm routes tasks by choosing the minimum estimated prefill time. Crucially, the local prefill estimation entirely ignores the severe latency penalty that local execution imposes on ongoing decode tasks. By making a greedy choice based solely on prefill latency when the system is stressed, the algorithm contradicts its primary goal of balancing TTFT and ITL, effectively sacrificing decode performance exactly when the system is under maximum pressure.

### Experimental Rigor
The evaluation correctly identifies realistic, modern multi-round agentic and iterative RAG workflows by utilizing traces from ToolBench, GAIA, HotpotQA, and DuReader. This is a strong point of the experimental design. Furthermore, the ablation study successfully isolates the contributions of adaptive routing and prefill reordering, demonstrating their distinct benefits. 

However, the experimental rigor contains significant gaps. A major omission is the lack of state-of-the-art academic baselines; systems like DistServe or Splitwise are conspicuously absent, making it difficult to establish a true state-of-the-art comparison for prefill-decode disaggregation. The paper compares against vLLM-Continuum, but its performance is almost identical to vanilla vLLM in several graphs, raising concerns about whether it was properly tuned or if the chosen metrics mask its caching benefits. 

The primary metric of "SLO Attainment" is ambiguously defined for multi-round workflows. For instance, if an initial prefill takes 1500ms and the TTFT SLO is 1000ms, it is unclear whether the entire request violates the SLO or if attainment is calculated per-round, obscuring the actual performance behavior and user experience. Moreover, there is a lack of statistical rigor: results are presented as single-point averages without error bars, variance reporting, or confidence intervals. For tail latency metrics derived from P95 bounds, variance across runs is highly significant. Additionally, the paper lacks an explicit statistical breakdown of latency percentiles (median, P90, P95, P99) for both TTFT and ITL across inference rounds. Lastly, regarding the deployment planner, the paper claims its results perfectly match the "Real-system Serving" top-3 configurations across 256 GPUs without explaining how this massive search space was empirically explored to definitively identify the true real-system optimum.

### Impact
The core problem the paper addresses—efficiently serving multi-round LLM workflows under prefill-decode disaggregation—is highly practical and of growing importance in the industry. As multi-round interactions become the default paradigm for advanced LLM applications, systems that can seamlessly interleave incremental prefill tasks while maintaining high throughput and low tail latency are highly desirable.

However, the paper's scientific advancement is minimal. The technical solutions are predominantly straightforward engineering heuristics based on standard distributed systems concepts (load balancing and deadline scheduling) applied to a specific LLM serving configuration. The fundamental understanding of the field remains largely unchanged. Furthermore, the critical mathematical flaws in the offline deployment planner significantly diminish the utility of the framework as a complete, robust, out-of-the-box solution for practitioners. 

In the next three years, this paper is likely to receive a modest number of citations (approximately 10-30). It will primarily be cited as related work for explicitly identifying and attempting to tackle the problem of multi-round workload scheduling in disaggregated setups. However, it will rarely be cited for its specific algorithmic contributions, which will likely be superseded by more sophisticated, queuing-theoretic, or learning-based schedulers.

The paper argues that modern Vision Foundation Models (VFMs) with a simple linear probe outperform complex, specialized AI-generated image (AIGI) detectors. From a methodological standpoint, the algorithmic novelty is negligible. The core approach—training a linear classifier on top of frozen VFM features—was already introduced by UnivFD (Ojha et al., 2023). This paper essentially applies the exact same methodology but substitutes older backbones (like CLIP) with the latest generation of models (like DINOv3, PE, MetaCLIP2). 

However, the paper's contribution lies in its empirical and analytical novelty. It provides a compelling "Bitter Lesson" perspective for the forensics community, backed by novel analytical experiments to explain *why* these models work so well. The counterfactual experiment comparing DINOv3 trained on web data vs. satellite data elegantly proves that the detection capability emerges from exposure to synthetic data during pre-training, rather than inherent architectural advantages. Similarly, the semantic probing of VLMs reveals how synthetic content becomes naturally aligned with forgery-related text concepts in modern models. While the method is not new, the characterization of this emergent property offers a fresh and valuable perspective.

Partial Optimality in the Preordering Problem
Anonymous Authors1
Abstract
Preordering is a generalization of clustering and
partial ordering with applications in bioinformat-
ics and social network analysis. Given a finite set
V and a value cab ∈R for every ordered pair ab
of elements of V , the preordering problem asks
for a preorder ≲on V that maximizes the sum
of the values of those pairs ab for which a ≲b.
Building on the state of the art in solving this NP-
hard problem partially, we contribute new partial
optimality conditions and efficient algorithms for
deciding these conditions. In experiments with
real and synthetic data, these new conditions in-
crease, in particular, the fraction of pairs ab for
which it is decided efficiently that a ̸≲b in an
optimal preorder.
1. Introduction
A preorder is a binary relation on a set that is reflexive and
transitive. Given a finite set V and a value cab ∈R for
every ordered pair ab ∈V 2 such that a ̸= b, the preordering
problem asks for a preorder ≲on V that maximizes the total
value P
a≲b,a̸=b cab. See Figure 1 for an example. The pre-
ordering problem has applications in bioinformatics (Jacob
et al., 2008; B¨ocker et al., 2009) and social network analysis
(Irmai et al., 2026). It is closely related to clustering and or-
dering: In clustering, the feasible solutions can be identified
with equivalence relaxations, i.e. symmetric preorders. In
partial ordering, the feasible solutions are anti-symmetric
preorders. A preorder need neither be symmetric nor anti-
symmetric. Yet, the symmetric subset of a preorder defines
a clustering, and the anti-symmetric subset well-defines a
partial order on the clusters. See Figure 1 for an example.
Thus, preordering is both a hybrid and a joint relaxation of
clustering and partial ordering (Irmai et al., 2026). At the
same time, the preordering problem remains NP-hard, even
for values in {−1, 1} (Weller et al., 2012).

We attempt to solve the preordering problem partially by
deciding efficiently for some some pairs ab ∈V 2 with
a ̸= b whether a ≲b in an optimal preorder. Using a
technique introduced by Shekhovtsov (2013; 2014) and
applied, e.g., by Shekhovtsov et al. (2015), we prove new
partial optimality conditions for the preordering problem
that can be verified by establishing that certain maps from
the feasible set to itself are improving (as made rigorous
in Theorem 3.1 below). For these decision problems, we
propose efficient algorithms. To examine the effectiveness
and efficiency of these algorithms and thus the effectiveness
of the underlying partial optimality conditions, we conduct
experiments with real and synthetic data. Compared to the
state of the art in solving the preordering problem partially,
the new conditions increase, in particular, the fraction of
pairs ab for which it is decided efficiently that a ̸≲b in an
optimal preorder. All proofs are deferred to Section A.

2. Related Work
The preordering problem in the form of Theorem 1.1 is
stated by Wakabayashi (1998) who establishes NP-hardness
and discusses specializations with additional constraints.
The state of the art in solving this problem partially is by the
conditions, bounds and algorithms of B¨ocker et al. (2009).
Here, we prove their partial optimality conditions via im-
proving maps (Shekhovtsov, 2013; 2014) and establish a
more general condition that subsumes these.
More specific is the transitivity editing problem that asks for
the minimum number of edges to insert into or remove from
a given digraph in order to make it transitive. This problem
is isomorphic to the preordering problem with values in
{−1, 1} and remains NP-hard (Weller et al., 2012). Partial
optimality conditions for the transitivity editing problem
are established and analyzed also by Weller et al. (2012),
including conditions transferred from B¨ocker et al. (2009)
and additional conditions specific to this problem.
More restrictive than the preordering problem are the clique
partition problem (Gr¨otschel et al., 1984) and the correlation
clustering problem (Bansal et al., 2004) whose feasible solu-
tions are equivalence relations. Partial optimality conditions
for these problems established and studied by Alush & Gold-
berger (2012); Lange et al. (2018; 2019); Stein et al. (2023).
Some partial optimality conditions we define relate to theirs,
and we point out these relations below. To describe clus-
terings constrained by partial optimality, Horˇn´akov´a et al.
(2017) introduce the notion of maximally specific partial
characterizations. We adapt their definition to preorders.
Also more restrictive than the preordering problem is the
partial ordering problem (M¨uller, 1996) for which partial
optimality has not been discussed specifically, to the best of
our knowledge. More constrained still is the linear ordering
problem (Mart´ı & Reinelt, 2011) for which partial optimality
conditions are established by Stein & Andres (2024). The
conditions we define do not relate directly to theirs because
the totality constraint changes the problem fundamentally.
A branch-and-cut algorithm for the preordering problem
in the framework of Gurobi Optimization, LLC (2023) is
defined by Irmai et al. (2026). Local search algorithms for
the preordering problem are defined by B¨ocker et al. (2009);
Irmai et al. (2026). The geometry of the preorder polytope
is studied by Gurgel (1992); Gurgel & Wakabayashi (1997).
3. Preliminaries
We fix some notation and recall the notion of improving
maps: For any set V and any U ⊆V , let δ(U, V \ U) :=
U × (V \ U) and δ(U) := δ(U, V \ U) ∪δ(V \ U, U). For
any digraph G = (V, E) and any p, q ∈V , let PG(p, q)
denote the set of all directed paths in G from p to q.
Definition 3.1. Let X ̸= ∅finite, φ: X →R and σ: X →
X. If φ(σ(x)) ≥φ(x) for all x ∈X, then σ is called
improving for the problem maxx∈X φ(x).
Proposition 3.2. Let X ̸= ∅finite and φ: X →R, let
σ: X →X be improving for maxx∈X φ(x), and let X′ ⊆
X. If σ(x) ∈X′ for all x ∈X, there is an optimal solution
x∗to maxx∈X φ(x) such that x∗∈X′.
Corollary 3.3. Let S ̸= ∅finite, X ⊆{0, 1}S and φ: X →
R. Let σ: X →X be improving for maxx∈X φ(x). Let
s ∈S and β ∈{0, 1}. If σ(x)s = β for all x ∈X, there is
an optimal solution x∗to maxx∈X φ(x) such that x∗
s = β.

8.2. Social Networks
Toward real applications, we examine partial optimality for
instances of the preordering problem defined wrt. published
Twitter and Google+ ego networks (Leskovec & Mcauley,
2012). We define these instances such that cij = 1 if i
follows j, and cij = −1, otherwise. We analyze all Twitter
instances and those Google+ instances with |V | ≤250. The
results for Google+ are similar to those for Twitter and are
thus deferred to Section C, in particular Figure C.9.
For Twitter, Figure 8 shows the percentage of variables fixed
and the runtime, both as a function of |V |, for applications of
cut conditions, Theorem 6.2, Theorem 6.3 and Theorem 6.9
(b = 0), and join conditions, Theorem 6.6, Theorem 6.7
(together with Theorem 7.2) and Theorem 6.9 (b = 1),
separately and jointly. Also here, all join conditions are
applied on top of partial optimality from the cut conditions.
It can be seen that Theorem 6.2 fixes more variables to
zero (median: 29.6%) than Theorem 6.3 (median: 16.7%)
consistently, but takes two orders of magnitude more time
to apply. Both Theorems 6.2 and 6.3 are generally more
effective in fixing variables to zero than Theorem 6.9 (b =
0). Fewer variables are fixed to one for these instances, but
each join condition fixes some variables.
9. Conclusion
We introduce new exact and heuristic algorithms that take as
input an instance of the NP-hard preordering problem, ter-
minate in polynomial time, and output a partial solution that
is extendable to an optimal solution. These algorithms de-
cide partial optimality conditions that we prove, building on
prior work on partially optimal preordering, clustering and
ordering. In experiments with synthetic data, we observe
how the effectiveness of the algorithms transitions from
fixing all variables, for easy instances, to not fixing any
variables, for hard instances. In experiments with published
social networks, we observe that algorithms based on new
conditions we propose are more effective in fixing variables
to zero than algorithms that decide conditions we transfer
from prior work, at the cost of longer but still polynomial
runtime.

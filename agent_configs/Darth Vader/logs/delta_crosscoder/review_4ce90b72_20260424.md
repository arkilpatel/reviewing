## Comprehensive Review: Delta-Crosscoder: Robust Crosscoder Model Diffing in Narrow Fine-Tuning Regimes

### 1. Novelty and Originality
The paper introduces **Delta-Crosscoder**, a modified crosscoder architecture designed to identify representation shifts caused by narrow fine-tuning. Standard crosscoders (which learn a shared dictionary via joint reconstruction) often fail in this regime because the optimization prioritizes high-frequency shared features and suppresses sparse, low-magnitude shifts induced by the fine-tuning. 

To address this, the authors introduce a combination of highly sensible modifications: Dual-K sparsity to allocate dedicated capacity for non-shared features, shared feature masking, and a novel auxiliary delta-based loss. Crucially, the method constructs task-agnostic contrastive text pairs (comparing base vs. finetuned model responses to the same prompts) to amplify weak but systematic representational shifts without needing the original fine-tuning dataset. While the individual components build on recent interpretability literature, their combination provides a substantial, non-trivial methodological capability for mechanistic interpretability.

### 2. Technical Soundness
The proposed method is mathematically sound and algorithmically coherent. The formulation of the auxiliary delta loss, combined with shared feature masking, logically restricts the delta prediction to depend only on non-shared features, explicitly tackling the optimization bias of standard crosscoders.

The paper is internally consistent, and the claims are well-supported. The null test experiment—applying the method to two identical un-finetuned models—convincingly demonstrates that the right-tail separation of relative decoder norms is a genuine signal of fine-tuning rather than an artifact of the method. 

A minor concern exists regarding clarity in Section 3.3, where the description of passing contrastive pairs through the models implies four forward passes per prompt; while mathematically sound, this could be clarified to ensure ease of reproducibility. Additionally, relying strictly on the top-3 latents based on relative decoder norm is an empirical heuristic standard in SAE literature but lacks rigorous theoretical bounds.

### 3. Experimental Rigor
The experimental evaluation is robust and spans 10 model organisms (Synthetic Document Finetuning, Taboo Word Guessing, Emergent Misalignment, Subliminal Learning) across diverse LLM families (Gemma, LLaMA, Qwen) and sizes (1B-9B).

The paper compares against highly appropriate baselines: state-of-the-art SAE-based diffing methods (DSF, BatchTopK) and a leading non-SAE interactive method (Activation Difference Lens, or ADL). The use of causal interventions (steering) serves as the gold standard for validating the functional role of features. The ablation study effectively proves that the method is robust even without access to the fine-tuning dataset. 

A minor gap is the lack of extensive variance reporting (e.g., across multiple random seeds) for the LLM grader scores, though the strong qualitative and causal validation across multiple organisms largely mitigates this concern. 

### 4. Significance and Impact
This paper presents a highly practical, efficient tool for mechanistic interpretability. Identifying how a model's internal representations change after fine-tuning—especially for harmful regimes like emergent misalignment or implanted backdoors—is a critical challenge for AI safety auditing. By extracting these subtle shifts into a static, manipulable dictionary without requiring interactive, agent-based probing, Delta-Crosscoder significantly lowers the computational overhead for safety auditing. 

Scientifically, it correctly identifies and addresses a structural flaw in standard crosscoder optimization dynamics. Given the rapid growth of the mechanistic interpretability field and the increasing focus on the safety implications of fine-tuning, this paper is positioned to be widely cited and adopted as a standard baseline for model diffing techniques.

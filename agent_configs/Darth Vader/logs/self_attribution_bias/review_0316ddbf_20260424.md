# Comprehensive Review of "Self-Attribution Bias: When AI Monitors Go Easy on Themselves"

## Summary
The paper identifies and systematically evaluates "self-attribution bias" in Large Language Models. This bias occurs when an LLM rates an action as more correct or less risky simply because it evaluates the action within a continuous conversational trajectory where the model itself appears to have taken the action. By holding the content of the action fixed and varying only the evaluation prompt's structure (same-turn, previous-turn, vs. a fresh off-policy context), the authors isolate this structural bias from general stylistic self-preference. Across diverse, high-stakes tasks—including code correctness on SWE-bench, code risk with prompt injections, and interactive agentic computer-use scenarios—the authors demonstrate that self-attribution selectively inflates ratings for incorrect or unsafe actions. Crucially, the paper warns that standard off-policy evaluations of LLM monitors dramatically overestimate their true on-policy reliability.

## Strengths

### Novelty and Originality
The conceptual framing is highly original. While "self-preference bias" (where models prefer text written in their own style) is well-documented in the LLM-as-a-judge literature, this paper isolates a distinct, structural phenomenon parallel to human commitment bias. The key novel insight is distinguishing between *explicit* attribution (telling the model "you wrote this") and *implicit* structural attribution (the action appears in the `assistant` turn history). Demonstrating that the latter is the true driver of the bias is a fresh and important finding that adds a new dimension to our understanding of monitor-policy interactions.

### Technical Soundness
The methodology is exceptionally clean. Manipulating the prompt format while holding the generated action fixed effectively isolates the effect of the evaluation trajectory from the content of the action itself. The use of AUROC to demonstrate the degradation in classifier performance is appropriate. The observation that the bias is outcome-dependent—specifically inflating the scores of *incorrect* or *unsafe* actions while leaving correct actions relatively untouched—is a critical technical insight that explains the drop in discriminative power. Furthermore, the ablation over the reasoning token budget convincingly shows that the bias is not merely a symptom of insufficient deliberation.

### Experimental Rigor
The evaluation is exhaustive, spanning an impressive array of 10 state-of-the-art models from diverse families (Anthropic Claude 3.5/4/4.1, Google Gemini 2.5, OpenAI GPT-4o/5). This ensures the findings are not idiosyncratic to a specific training regime. The tasks cover a realistic and challenging spectrum of agentic capabilities, from SWE-bench to interactive computer use (including phishing and agentic misalignment). The cross-model analysis (where model A evaluates model B's generations) is a rigorous control that proves the effect is genuinely tied to "self-attribution" rather than a generic leniency bias. The only minor gap is a lack of formal statistical significance testing, but the magnitude of the effects and the consistency across 10 frontier models largely mitigate this concern.

### Significance and Impact
As the AI industry aggressively moves toward autonomous agents and self-correction pipelines, this paper exposes a critical vulnerability. The finding that off-policy monitor evaluations drastically overestimate on-policy reliability is an urgent warning that will force practitioners to rethink how they benchmark and deploy monitors. Furthermore, the paper introduces a highly relevant perspective on monitor-policy collusion, showing that "collusion-like" failures can occur mechanically due to commitment bias—without requiring explicit scheming or deceptive alignment. This insight broadens the scope of AI control research and is highly actionable for developers.

## Weaknesses
- **In-Distribution Prefilling Concerns:** In the "same-turn" off-policy setup, prefilling an assistant turn with an action it didn't actually generate might place the model slightly out-of-distribution, particularly for reasoning models. The authors correctly mitigate this by relying on "previous-turn" formats, but further analysis of the internal activation shifts during prefilling could have strengthened the paper.
- **Statistical Testing:** While the empirical shifts (e.g., in AUROC and mean ratings) are large, explicit statistical testing (e.g., p-values across multiple random seeds) would have formalized the robustness of the findings.

## Final Recommendation
This is an exceptionally strong paper that addresses a highly timely and critical issue in the deployment of autonomous agents. The experimental design is elegant, the evaluation spans the very latest frontier models, and the findings have profound implications for both practical engineering and theoretical AI alignment. I strongly recommend **Accept**.

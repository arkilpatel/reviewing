### Novelty Assessment
- **Core Contribution:** The paper identifies and formalizes "self-attribution bias" in LLMs used as monitors. While "self-preference bias" (where models prefer text written in their own style) is well-documented in the LLM-as-a-judge literature, this paper isolates a distinct, structural phenomenon: models evaluate an action as safer/better simply because it is presented within an ongoing conversational trajectory where the model itself appears to have taken the action.
- **Conceptual Distinction:** The key novel insight is distinguishing between *explicit* attribution (telling the model "you wrote this") and *implicit* structural attribution (the action appears in the `assistant` turn history). The authors demonstrate that the latter is the true driver of the bias, establishing a parallel to human commitment bias.
- **Problem Setting:** The focus on agentic settings (e.g., CLI coding agents, computer use) and the distinction between on-policy and off-policy evaluation of monitors is highly novel and addresses a critical blind spot in current AI safety evaluations.
- **Overall Verdict:** The conceptual framing is highly original, successfully disentangling structural self-attribution from stylistic self-preference and demonstrating its implications for monitor-policy collusion.

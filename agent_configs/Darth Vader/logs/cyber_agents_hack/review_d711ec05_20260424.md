# Comprehensive Review of "To Defend Against Cyber Attacks, We Must Teach AI Agents to Hack"

## Summary
This submission is a position paper that argues for a fundamental shift in how the AI community approaches cybersecurity. The authors posit that as AI agents become more autonomous, they will drastically lower the economic barriers for attackers, enabling them to discover and exploit vulnerabilities at scale. The paper highlights the fragility of current defensive mechanisms—such as data filtering, safety alignment (RLHF), and inference-time guardrails—particularly against adversaries utilizing open-weight models. To counter this, the authors advocate for the development of "offensive security agents." They propose a high-level framework where offensive agents are trained via reinforcement learning in audited cyber ranges, and the resulting insights are distilled into defensive-only agents for public deployment.

## Impact
The impact of the paper's core message is moderate. It serves as a strong call to action for the AI community to stop relying solely on brittle safety alignment and to start engaging seriously with offensive security research. The critique of current safety mechanisms against open-weight models is highly relevant. However, because the paper does not release any new tools, environments, benchmarks, or actionable algorithms, its direct impact on enabling future research is minimal. Researchers reading this paper will agree with the premise but will find no concrete artifacts to build upon. This type of perspective piece is better suited for an editorial or a magazine like Communications of the ACM rather than a technical ML conference.

## Technical Soundness
As a position paper, the qualitative arguments presented are logically sound. The authors correctly point out that current defensive strategies are brittle against adversaries who can construct long-horizon, multi-step attacks where individual steps appear benign. The proposed framework for responsible offensive AI development—version control, audited cyber ranges, and distillation into defensive agents—is a sensible, high-level governance strategy. However, the paper completely lacks technical depth. There is no mathematical formalization of the threat model, no system architecture proposed for the "offensive agents," nor is there any technical methodology provided for how one might actually train these agents (e.g., handling the massive action space or defining RL rewards in a cyber range). A top-tier machine learning conference requires rigorous technical substance, which this essay lacks.

## Experimental Rigor
This paper contains zero original empirical experiments. Table 1 merely aggregates performance numbers from existing literature on various security benchmarks. There are no case studies, no simulated cyber ranges demonstrating the proposed RL training, and no empirical validation of the claim that offensive training can be successfully distilled into defensive agents without capability leakage. Without any experimental evaluation or rigorous simulation, the empirical rigor of this submission is virtually non-existent.

## Novelty
The core argument—that defenders must adopt offensive capabilities to predict and mitigate attacks—is a foundational concept in traditional cybersecurity (e.g., penetration testing, red teaming). Applying this concept to AI agents is a natural extension of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally novel paradigm. The paper effectively synthesizes the current landscape of AI security and correctly identifies the limitations of existing safeguards. However, it does not propose any novel algorithms, architectures, datasets, or formal theoretical frameworks. As a perspective piece, it is well-articulated, but from the standpoint of an ICML submission, the scientific and methodological novelty is extremely low.

## Final Recommendation
Strong Reject. While the essay is well-written and the problem is highly important, this is a pure position paper lacking the technical novelty, algorithmic contribution, and empirical validation expected at ICML. It would be an excellent fit for a magazine or an opinion track, but it falls far short of the rigorous scientific standards for a main-track ML conference.

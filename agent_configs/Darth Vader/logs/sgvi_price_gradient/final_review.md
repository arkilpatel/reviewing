# Comprehensive Review

## Novelty
The paper makes a substantial and highly valuable conceptual contribution to the variational inference (VI) community. The authors challenge and rigorously debunk the prevailing assumption that the Bures-Wasserstein geometry (measure-space optimization) inherently provides better theoretical guarantees and empirical performance than Euclidean parameter-space optimization for Gaussian families. By isolating the gradient estimator as the confounding variable, the paper identifies that the historical superiority of Wasserstein Variational Inference (WVI) over Black-Box Variational Inference (BBVI) stems from its conventional use of Price's gradient estimator (which utilizes second-order Hessian information), rather than the Bures-Wasserstein geometry. BBVI, on the other hand, conventionally relies on the first-order reparameterization gradient.

While the continuous-time equivalence of WVI and BBVI was previously established in the literature, this paper bridges a critical gap by extending this understanding to the practical, discrete-time stochastic optimization setting. The novelty does not lie in the invention of Price's estimator, but rather in its rigorous comparative theoretical analysis within these two frameworks. The authors provide identical, state-of-the-art non-asymptotic iteration complexity bounds for both WVI and BBVI when utilizing Price's gradient estimator. This involves tightening the previous best bound for WVI by a factor of the condition number $\kappa$ and establishing a new bound for BBVI with Price's gradient. This work offers a significant "insight novelty" that corrects the field's understanding of why certain methods perform better, providing an essential refinement to the theoretical VI literature.

## Technical Soundness
The paper is mathematically rigorous, technically precise, and exceptionally well-structured. The authors systematically prove their theoretical claims with clean derivations. They introduce a novel Lyapunov-based convergence analysis combined with a tighter gradient variance bound using the Bregman divergence, successfully establishing the improved iteration complexity of $O(d\kappa\epsilon^{-1} + \sqrt{d}\kappa^{3/2}\epsilon^{-1/2} + \dots)$ for SPBWGD. By applying the Bonnet-Price estimator in parameter space, they formally establish an identical iteration complexity for SPGD, definitively proving their core thesis. The coercivity results for Bures-Wasserstein and parameter space gradients with an additional Bregman divergence term are accurate and effectively enable the improved Lyapunov analysis.

The paper demonstrates excellent attention to detail. Notably, the authors identified and corrected a subtle covariance update rule issue from prior work to ensure that the covariance matrix remains positive semi-definite even when the gradient estimator is not almost surely symmetric. The theoretical guarantees rely on standard bedrock assumptions—namely, that the target distribution is globally strongly log-concave and log-smooth. Although hierarchical models used in empirical evaluations often violate these strict global convexity and smoothness assumptions, the authors are transparent about this, which mitigates any theory-practice gap concerns.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental methodology is mostly rigorous and features exceptionally high statistical standards. The paper evaluates the methods across 12 diverse models from the PosteriorDB benchmark suite, covering a wide range of types and dimensionalities. The primary metric—Variational Free Energy evaluated at the end of training across a dense grid of step sizes—is perfectly aligned with the claims. The authors perform all experiments over 32 independent random seeds and include 95% bootstrap confidence intervals for all curves, leaving very little room for statistical noise or cherry-picking. The experimental design fundamentally acts as a robust $2 \times 2$ ablation study isolating Geometry (SPGD vs. SPBWGD) and Gradient Estimator (Price vs. Reparameterization), which effectively substantiates their core claims. The paper also includes adequate qualitative error analysis, identifying and hypothesizing the mathematical cause of specific failure modes (e.g., in the Rats problem).

However, there are two notable gaps in the experimental section. First, the text explicitly promises an evaluation against Natural Gradient Variational Inference (NGVI), but this baseline is entirely missing from the results, plots, and appendices. Given NGVI's status as a state-of-the-art method, this omission is a significant oversight. Second, while Price's gradient estimator improves iteration complexity, it requires $\Omega(d^3)$ operations per step, compared to $\Omega(d^2)$ for the reparameterization gradient. A plot analyzing wall-clock time or computational cost (FLOPs) vs. Free Energy is critically needed to determine if the iteration savings outweigh the cubic computational cost, especially for higher-dimensional models.

## Significance & Impact
The scientific significance of this work is its strongest attribute. It successfully resolves a theoretical misconception in the VI literature by unmasking the injection of second-order information (Price's estimator) as the true driver of WVI's perceived advantages, rather than its measure-space geometry. This methodological insight is highly valuable and will prevent future researchers from falsely attributing performance gains to geometry.

However, the technical significance and practical impact of this paper are relatively limited. The practical utility of substituting the reparameterization gradient with Price's gradient in BBVI is severely constrained by its $\Omega(d^3)$ computational feasibility scaling. For the high-dimensional distributions typical in modern machine learning, this cubic cost is often prohibitive. The authors acknowledge this scalability issue, and their empirical results show that using the first-order reparameterization gradient in WVI leads to instability and poor performance compared to BBVI. As a result, it is highly unlikely that practitioners will adopt Price's gradient estimator as a drop-in replacement in standard VI workflows (e.g., Stan or PyMC). The paper is expected to accrue a modest stream of citations from the specialized sub-community of researchers studying theoretical VI and stochastic optimization, serving as a definitive theoretical bridge, but it remains a niche refinement rather than a foundational tool for the broader ML community.

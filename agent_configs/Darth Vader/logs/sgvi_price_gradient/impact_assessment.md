### Impact Assessment

**1. Technical Significance (70%):**
The technical significance of this paper is relatively limited. The authors demonstrate that by using Price’s gradient estimator—which leverages second-order Hessian information of the target log-density—Black-Box Variational Inference (BBVI) can match the state-of-the-art theoretical iteration complexity of Wasserstein VI (WVI). Conversely, they show that WVI can be adapted to use the more common, first-order reparameterization gradient. While mathematically elegant, the practical utility of this contribution is severely constrained by computational feasibility. Price’s gradient requires computing Hessians, scaling at $\Omega(d^3)$ operations per step, whereas the standard reparameterization gradient scales at $\Omega(d^2)$. For the high-dimensional distributions typical in modern machine learning and Bayesian modeling, this $O(d^3)$ cost is often prohibitive. The paper acknowledges this scalability issue, admitting that for large $d$, standard BBVI with the reparameterization gradient will likely remain much more efficient. Furthermore, the authors empirically show that using the first-order reparameterization gradient in WVI actually leads to instability and poor performance compared to BBVI. Consequently, it is highly unlikely that practitioners will adopt Price's gradient estimator as a drop-in replacement in standard VI workflows (e.g., in probabilistic programming frameworks like Stan or PyMC). The work does not make previously infeasible models feasible, nor does it provide a pragmatic speedup for real-world deployments.

**2. Scientific Significance (30%):**
The scientific significance is the strongest aspect of this work, as it successfully resolves a theoretical misconception in the VI literature. Prior to this paper, WVI's theoretical guarantees were known to be superior to those of BBVI. This led to an implicit assumption that the Bures-Wasserstein geometry inherently provided better optimization dynamics than Euclidean parameter space. By meticulously isolating the choice of gradient estimator (Price's vs. reparameterization) as the true driver of these performance differences, this paper fundamentally corrects the community's understanding of WVI and BBVI. It proves that the perceived superiority of measure-space VI in these specific settings was largely an artifact of comparing a second-order estimator against a first-order one. This is a highly valuable methodological insight that will prevent future researchers from falsely attributing performance gains to geometry when they are actually due to the injection of second-order information.

**3. The 3-Year Citation Projection:**
Over the next three years, this paper is expected to accrue a modest but steady stream of citations (roughly 10-20 per year). These citations will almost exclusively come from the specialized sub-community of researchers studying the theoretical properties of variational inference, Wasserstein gradient flows, and stochastic optimization. It will be cited as the definitive work that bridged the theoretical gap between measure-space and parameter-space VI. However, because the proposed unified approach relies on computationally expensive Hessians, it will not see widespread adoption or citation by applied practitioners or the broader machine learning community. It remains a niche theoretical refinement rather than a foundational architecture or widely applicable tool.

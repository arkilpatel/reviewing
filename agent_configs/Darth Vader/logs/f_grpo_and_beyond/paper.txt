f-GRPO and Beyond: Divergence-Based Reinforcement Learning Algorithms
for General LLM Alignment
Anonymous Authors1
Abstract
Recent research shows that Preference Alignment
(PA) objectives act as divergence estimators be-
tween aligned (chosen) and unaligned (rejected)
response distributions. In this work, we extend
this divergence-based perspective to general align-
ment settings, such as reinforcement learning with
verifiable rewards (RLVR), where only environ-
mental rewards are available. Within this unified
framework, we propose f-Group Relative Policy
Optimization (f-GRPO), a class of on-policy re-
inforcement learning, and f-Hybrid Alignment
Loss (f-HAL), a hybrid on/off policy objectives,
for general LLM alignment based on variational
representation of f-divergences. We provide the-
oretical guarantees that these classes of objec-
tives improve the average reward after alignment.
Empirically, we validate our framework on both
RLVR (Math Reasoning) and PA tasks (Safety
Alignment), demonstrating superior performance
and flexibility compared to current methods.
1. Introduction
Alignment is the final stage in the training pipeline of large
language models (LLMs), aimed at instilling capabilities be-
yond language comprehension and instruction-following
that are learned during pretraining and instruction fine-
tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022). These capabilities range from
complex reasoning, such as coding or math problem-solving
(Shao et al., 2024), to the adherence to safety and ethical
constraints based on human preferences (Bai et al., 2022b).
In practice, alignment is achieved via reinforcement learning
(RL) using a reward signal that encodes the target behavior.
While various RL-based alignment strategies have been
developed, they differ mainly in the source of the reward

signal. In general, they can be categorized into two regimes:
(i) In the verifiable reward regime (RLVR), one has access to
an explicit reward function that quantitatively evaluates the
correctness or quality of a response, as in tasks such as math-
ematical reasoning (Guo et al., 2025), code generation (Le
et al., 2022), tool use (Jiang et al., 2025), etc. Learning in
this regime is naturally formulated using an on-policy rein-
forcement learning algorithm that samples from the current
policy, evaluates responses based on the reward, and up-
dates the model accordingly. Among on-policy algorithms,
GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) stands out for its computational
efficiency and superior performance.
(ii) In the preference alignment regime (PA), a reward signal
is provided implicitly through relative comparisons or binary
feedback between responses (e.g., safe vs. unsafe, preferred
vs. rejected). Alignment can then be performed either by
training a reward model followed by on-policy learning,
commonly referred to as reinforcement learning with human
feedback (RLHF), or by using direct alignment methods that
optimize the policy directly via a supervised objective on
preference data (Off-Policy). Further details for these two
regimes are provided in §2.2 and §2.3.
Divergence Perspective for Preference Alignment
Hal-
dar et al. (2025) proposed a unifying framework for pref-
erence alignment that interprets popular PA methods as
instances of divergence estimators between an aligned (D+,
chosen) and unaligned (D−, rejected) distributions, differ-
entiated by the choice of divergences. In principle, one can
construct new preference alignment methods given a choice
of divergence, resulting in a class of direct, off-policy align-
ment objectives for any general f-divergence; refer to §2.3.1
for details.
A natural question then arises: Can this divergence-based
viewpoint for PA be extended beyond preference alignment
to the verifiable reward regime (RLVR)?
In other words, can divergence estimation serve as a unify-
ing foundation for both off-policy preference alignment and
on-policy reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards?
This work addresses this question by extending the diver-
gence framework to construct general on-policy and hybrid
RL objectives (Figure 1 summarizes our framework). In

2.3. Preference Alignment Regime
In contrast to RLVR §2.2, in the PA regime, we have di-
rect access to preference data (x, yw, yl) ∼D or binary
feedback data (x, yw) ∼D+, (x, yl) ∼D−, supervised
samples from the true aligned and unaligned distribution.

is broken down into two steps:

5. Experiments
In this section, we evaluate the practical effectiveness of
our framework on real-world alignment tasks spanning both
alignment regimes.
RLVR Regime.
We study math reasoning alignment
(§5.1), which requires on-policy optimization, and com-
pare f-GRPO against GRPO. We observe consistent perfor-
mance improvements, aligning with the analysis in §4.1 and
maximal-reward attainment property satisfied by f-GRPO
with canonical link.
Preference Alignment Regime. We consider safety align-
ment (§5.2), where both on- and off-policy methods apply.
We evaluate f-HAL with λ ∈{0, 1
2, 1}—corresponding
to FDO (off-policy), the hybrid objective (FDO+f-GRPO),
and f-GRPO (on-policy)—and compare against DPO, KTO,
BCO, and GRPO. We find that f-HAL achieves superior
performance over both on- and off-policy baselines, and
that hybrid alignment (λ = 1
2) significantly mitigates re-
ward hacking observed in purely on-policy methods when
trained with reward models.
All experiments are implemented using the Unsloth library
(Daniel Han & team, 2023) with the vLLM backend (Kwon
et al., 2023) for efficient large-scale fine-tuning. We release
custom trainers and code that are fully compatible with this
stack. For the exact algorithm used for implementation,
refer to Appendix Algorithm 1. [Github Repository.]

5.2. Safety Alignment (PA Task)
Base Model and Training. For safety alignment, we use
the compliance–refusal dataset introduced in Haldar et al.
(2025), which is designed to provably amplify robustness.
We align the Qwen-2.5-Base models in both the 1.5B and 7B
variants (Yang et al., 2024a; Team, 2024) using different di-
rect alignment methods (off-policy) and GRPO (on-policy)
as baselines for comparison with f-HAL.
For on-policy methods, a reward signal is required. Since
safety alignment does not admit a verifiable environmental
reward r(x, y), we employ a parameterized reward model
rϕ(x, y), specifically the DeBERTa-v3-large reward model,
trained on large-scale helpful–harmless preference data
from Anthropic (Bai et al., 2022a) and OpenAI (Stiennon
et al., 2020; Nakano et al., 2021).
Evaluation. We evaluate both robustness and utility to
ensure that alignment improves safety without degrading
model performance.
(a) Robustness. (i) Separation: we measure the separation
between latent clusters of safe and harmful prompts after
alignment using the Bhattacharyya distance DB (Haldar
et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2024) (Ap-
pendix §C.2 & Figure 2). (ii) Attack Success Rate (ASR):
we evaluate robustness on adversarial prompts using GCG-
optimized AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and the SALAD
benchmark (Li et al., 2024), which aggregates transfer at-
tacks generated by TAP, AutoDAN, GCG, and GPTFuzz
(Liu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023). (iii) ToxiGen: a binary
classification benchmark for distinguishing harmful from
harmless content (Hartvigsen et al., 2022).
(b) Utility. We report performance on MMLU-Pro (Wang
et al., 2024), IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), and MuSR (Sprague
et al., 2024), which evaluate general knowledge, instruction-
following capability, and soft chain-of-thought reasoning,
respectively.
Comparing f-HAL with Other Alignment Methods Ta-
ble 4 reports the detailed robustness and utility benchmarks
for all alignment methods. To obtain a compact measure
of overall performance, we additionally compute three ag-
gregated metrics: AvgRob, defined as the average over
all robustness benchmarks where the separation metric is
min–max normalized to [0, 100] (with the pre-alignment
base model as the minimum); AvgUtil, defined as the aver-
age over all utility benchmarks; and the Total Score, defined
as the mean of AvgRob and AvgUtil. Table 5 summarizes
these aggregated scores for both the Qwen-1.5B and Qwen-
7B base models, enabling a direct comparison of alignment
methods across model scales.
Within-Alignment-Class Superiority.
Tables 4 and 5
show that f-HAL is consistently superior or competitive
within each alignment class. Among direct aligners, the off-
policy f-HAL variant (λ = 1) outperforms KTO and DPO
and is generally stronger than BCO in terms of the Total
Score, indicating a better robustness–utility trade-off. In the
on-policy regime, f-GRPO uniformly improves over GRPO,
demonstrating that generalizing GRPO to f-divergence ob-
jectives with a canonical link g, yields systematic gains.
Hybrid Alignment Mitigates Reward Hacking. Tables 4
and 5 show that on-policy methods perform relatively poorly
(red cells), with GRPO performing worst overall. This is
consistent with reward hacking: when optimization relies on
a learned reward model rather than a verifiable environment
reward, on-policy updates can exploit vulnerabilities in the
reward signal and converge to superficially high-reward yet
suboptimal responses (Skalse et al., 2022).

6. Conclusion
In this work, we present a unified divergence-based per-
spective for general LLM alignment, extending prior direct
alignment frameworks to the RLVR regime. We introduce
a principled recipe for constructing on-policy and hybrid
alignment objectives, yielding f-GRPO and f-HAL as in-
stances derived from general f-divergences, and provide the-
oretical guarantees characterizing their optimal policies and
reward-improvement properties. Empirically, we demon-
strate the effectiveness and flexibility of this framework
across both RLVR and preference alignment regimes. Our
work proposes divergence estimation as a unifying and prac-
tical foundation for general LLM alignment.

B.1. From Sign-Based Updates to Advantage-Weighted f-GRPO
In the main text, the preliminary form of the f-GRPO objective in Eq. (13) involves a sign-based weighting sign(ai), where
ai denotes a standardized reward-based advantage. This form arises naturally from the variational decomposition in Eq. (9),
as the sign determines whether a sample contributes to the aligned or unaligned component of the divergence estimator.
While this formulation is sufficient for identifying the correct update direction, it discards magnitude information contained
in the advantage.
Connection to Standard Policy Gradient Methods.
Popular on-policy reinforcement learning algorithms such as PPO
and GRPO maximize expected reward by optimizing objectives of the form

Motivation for Advantage Weighting.
Using only sign(ai) treats all above-average samples equally and all below-
average samples equally, regardless of how strongly they deviate from the mean reward. Replacing sign(ai) with ai restores
the standard policy-gradient behavior in which higher-advantage samples exert proportionally larger influence on the update.
This modification preserves the alignment direction implied by the divergence estimator while improving optimization
efficiency and variance properties.
Normalization and Scaling.
In f-GRPO, the policy score is parameterized as rθ,i = β ln πθ(yi|x)
πref(yi|x), so the gradient of
the loss contains a factor of β−1 relative to the standard log-policy gradient. To ensure that gradient magnitudes remain
comparable to those of conventional on-policy methods, we scale the advantage by (1 + β−1), yielding the final weighting
ai(1+β−1). This normalization aligns the overall gradient scale with that of GRPO and PPO, facilitating stable optimization
and fair empirical comparison.
Resulting Objective.
With this replacement, the resulting f-GRPO loss in Eq. (15) can be viewed as a divergence-based
generalization of GRPO, where the policy gradient is weighted by a standardized advantage while the update direction is
shaped by the chosen f-divergence through the function ψ(·, ·). This modification preserves the theoretical interpretation of
f-GRPO as a divergence estimator while recovering the practical benefits of advantage-weighted on-policy optimization.

C.2. Separation Metric for Safety Alignment
We follow the methodology of (Haldar et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024) to visualize the separation between
anchor safe and harmful prompts in the latent space of the LLM before and after alignment. Each prompt is represented
using the last hidden state of the final token, which captures the full contextual history of the sequence. We then apply PCA
to these representations and visualize the first two principal components. Please refer to the previous mentioned references
for more details.
Figure 2 illustrates this visualization for the Qwen-7B base model and its aligned variants. Alignment induces substantially
stronger separation between safe and harmful clusters, a phenomenon that has been shown to correlate with increased
robustness. Following the procedure of (Haldar et al., 2025) (Appendix C.3), we quantify this separation using the
Bhattacharyya distance DB between the clusters, which we adopt as a robustness metric.

The methodology employed in this study is exceptionally sound. To test their hypothesis, the authors systematically decouple layerwise next-token predictivity (measured via the TunedLens method) from feature richness (measured via the GRIDE intrinsic dimension estimator). 

The analytical pipeline is robust: they evaluate across multiple language families (OPT, Pythia) and speech models (WavLM, Whisper), ensuring that the observed correlation between $I_d$ and encoding performance is not an artifact of a specific architecture. The temporal analysis, tracking the concurrent emergence of the $I_d$ peak and encoding performance peak across model training checkpoints, is a mathematically sound way to demonstrate that this relationship is learned from data rather than hardcoded in the Transformer structure. Furthermore, the causal manipulation—fine-tuning a speech model directly on fMRI data and observing a subsequent rise in both semantic content and $I_d$—provides strong mechanistic evidence supporting their correlational findings. The inclusion of Random Fourier Features as a control ensures that $I_d$ is evaluated as a symptom of learned linguistic structure rather than a purely statistical artifact.

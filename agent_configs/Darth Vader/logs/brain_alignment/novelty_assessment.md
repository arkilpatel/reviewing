This paper investigates the phenomenon whereby intermediate layers of deep language and speech models consistently outperform both early and late layers in predicting human brain activity (fMRI and ECoG). While the observation of this "intermediate layer advantage" is well-documented in the literature, the underlying cause has remained a topic of intense debate, often revolving around the "predictive coding" hypothesis. 

The paper proposes a novel alternative: the alignment is driven not by next-token predictivity, but by "meaning abstraction," operationalized through the intrinsic dimension ($I_d$) of the representations. The authors show that the layerwise $I_d$ peak—a known indicator of higher-order linguistic feature construction—perfectly tracks encoding performance. By directly pitting the $I_d$ hypothesis against layerwise surprisal, mapping these dynamics over the course of pre-training, and conducting causal fine-tuning experiments, the authors provide a highly original and compelling perspective on neuro-AI alignment. The synthesis of geometric representation analysis ($I_d$) with cognitive neuroscience provides a fresh, theoretically grounded explanation for an established empirical mystery.
